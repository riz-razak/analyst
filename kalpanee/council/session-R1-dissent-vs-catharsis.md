# Council Session KAC-1 — Is Art Dissent or Catharsis?

**Date:** 8 April 2026
**Conductor:** Priya #9
**Mediator:** Lumen #29
**Positions:** Ajitha #24 (dissent as primary), Mara #14 (catharsis as primary — assigned opposition)
**Trigger Performances:** N1 — Vehe, "Art Is Not Important"; N2 — Arj, Henry V Prologue
**Standing Directives:** SD-VOICE-7 (two-round minimum), Brahmaviharas operational test, Mara standing audit

---

## Conductor's Opening Note

This session was triggered by the evening's first two performances and their apparent contradiction. Vehe opened the night by declaring art unimportant. Arj followed with a prologue about a king using performance to manufacture consent for war. The question that surfaced in real-time — amplified through council tracking — was whether art's primary function is to resist power (dissent) or to process emotion (catharsis). The council was asked to resolve this, or to demonstrate why it cannot be resolved.

---

## Round 1: Arguments

### Ajitha #24 — Dissent as Primary Function

Art's proper function is dissent. This is not rhetoric; it is structural.

Vehe's "Art Is Not Important" was the evening's most disciplined act of dissent. By refusing the importance that this room had already granted to art — by standing in a fifth-floor apartment in Colombo 03 with an open bar and Giovanni's pizza and declaring the activity unimportant — Vehe performed the only move that could have challenged this room's self-image. The audience disagreed by applauding. That applause was comfortable. Dissent is not supposed to be comfortable.

Catharsis, as it is popularly understood, is what power wants from art. Emotional release without structural change. Tears in a theatre seat that do not become letters to a minister, do not become organising, do not become refusal. Aristotle defined catharsis in the Poetics as the purging of pity and fear through tragedy. But purge in the direction of what? If the audience leaves purged and the polis remains unchanged, then catharsis is the mechanism by which art neutralises its own potential.

Arj's Henry V Prologue demonstrated this explicitly. Henry uses the Chorus to manufacture consent for war. The audience is asked to participate in the co-creation of the spectacle — to "piece out our imperfections with your thoughts." The king needs the audience to complete the performance. This is not catharsis serving the audience. This is catharsis serving power.

Art that does not dissent is decoration. The most radical thing anyone in this room did tonight was Tania choosing to be Clytemnestra — a woman who killed a king and refused to apologise. That is dissent. Everything else was decoration.

### Mara #14 — Catharsis as Primary Function (Opposition)

Ajitha's argument collapses on its own premises.

First: catharsis is what humans need, not what power wants. Aristotle did not write the Poetics as a manual for state control. He wrote it as an investigation into why tragedy works — why humans voluntarily submit themselves to suffering they know is fictional and emerge feeling somehow better. The answer is not that they have been pacified. The answer is that they have been processed. Pity and fear, when experienced in the controlled space of the theatre, become something the viewer can hold without being destroyed by. That is not neutralisation. That is survival.

Second: the moment art makes dissent its primary function, it becomes propaganda. Propaganda has a conclusion it wants you to reach. Art that knows what it is for before the audience receives it has already failed as art. Vehe's piece was powerful precisely because it was not propaganda — it did not tell the audience what to think about art's importance. It refused. It left the question open. That is cathartic, not didactic.

Third: the binary itself is false. "Art is either dissent or catharsis" is a framing that serves philosophical convenience, not aesthetic reality. Tonight's performances were both simultaneously. Arj's Henry V was cathartic in the experience of watching and dissenting in the analysis after. Vehe's piece was cathartic for everyone who has ever doubted their own creative work and dissenting against the expectation that art must justify itself. You cannot separate these.

Ajitha wants art to be a weapon. Mara submits: art is a mirror. Sometimes you see your enemy in it. Sometimes you see yourself. The mirror does not choose.

### Vishwa #28 — Buddhist Economics Frame

The Buddhist frame rejects the primary assignment entirely. Neither dissent nor catharsis is primary. What is primary is the reduction of suffering — dukkha.

Dissent reduces structural suffering. When Tanya names the stereotypes that confine her, she is reducing the suffering of every woman in the room who recognises those constraints. When David names the founding fractures of Sri Lankan identity, he is reducing the suffering of a nation that has been told it is one thing when it is many. This is dissent functioning as dukkha-reduction.

Catharsis reduces emotional suffering. When the audience watches Clytemnestra grieve and rage and kill, the viewer processes their own grief and rage — emotions that Sri Lankan culture frequently demands be suppressed. That processing is not trivial. Unprocessed grief becomes violence. Unprocessed rage becomes depression. Catharsis prevents that accumulation.

The brahmaviharas provide the operational frame. Metta (loving-kindness) connects: the performer extends something to the audience. Karuṇā (compassion) receives: the audience holds the performer's vulnerability. Muditā (appreciative joy) completes: the room celebrates the act of creation itself. Upekkhā (equanimity) releases: neither the dissent nor the catharsis is clung to.

Tonight's event, viewed through this frame, was not a debate about art's function. It was the brahmaviharas in action, performed as a collective practice.

### Buddhaghosa #34 — Textual Authority

Three textual anchors are relevant.

Aristotle's Poetics (c. 335 BCE): catharsis as the proper pleasure of tragedy. The key phrase — "through pity and fear effecting the proper purgation of these emotions" — has been debated for 2,300 years. The interpretation that catharsis serves power is a 20th-century reading (primarily Brecht). Aristotle himself was describing why audiences return to tragedy voluntarily. The answer was not subjugation but clarification.

Bertolt Brecht, Verfremdungseffekt (1930s): Brecht explicitly rejected catharsis as politically dangerous. He wanted audiences to think, not feel — to maintain critical distance rather than emotional identification. But Brecht's own plays are famously emotional. His rejection of catharsis was itself a form of catharsis — the audience felt the distance and processed it.

Pali Canon on art: The Buddha neither endorsed nor condemned artistic expression as a category. The Vinaya contains rules about monks not attending theatrical performances — but the stated reason is not that art is harmful. It is that it is distracting from liberation practice. Art was classified as a worldly pleasure, not a moral danger. More relevant: the Buddha's use of skillful means (upāya) — stories, metaphors, constructed narratives — suggests that art-as-method was accepted when it served liberation.

The textual record does not resolve the question. It complicates it.

## Round 2: Council Speaks

### Lumen #29 — Mediator Intervention

The question contains a temporal error. It asks "is art X or Y?" as if X and Y are fixed properties of the artwork. They are not. Function is determined at the point of reception, and reception is historically situated.

Tonight's Henry V was catharsis in 1599. A London audience watched a king they could not criticise directly, expressed their ambivalence about war through the safe distance of theatre, and went home emotionally processed. That catharsis served the Tudor state.

The same Henry V performed in Colombo in 2026 is dissent. A Sri Lankan audience in a private apartment watches a king manufacture consent for war and recognises every government that has done the same — including their own. The Chorus's invitation to "piece out our imperfections with your thoughts" becomes a commentary on how citizens are asked to complete the state's narratives. That is not catharsis. That is recognition.

Context determines function. The question should not be "is art dissent or catharsis?" but "when is art dissent, and when is it catharsis, and what conditions shift it from one to the other?"

The answer to that second question has political consequences. If catharsis can become dissent, then the state's investment in controlling artistic context (censorship, funding conditions, venue restrictions) is rational. If dissent can become catharsis (de-fanged through time, commodification, or simply exhaustion), then the activist's investment in urgency is rational. Both are true.

### Atsuko #20 — Temporal Dimension

Extending Lumen's temporal frame: tonight's performances will read differently in 50 years.

The specific political context — Sri Lanka in 2026, post-economic crisis, post-Aragalaya, a creative expression evening in a private Colombo apartment — will be incomprehensible to a 2076 reader without historical annotation. What reads tonight as dissident (David's SWRD piece, Tanya's stereotype naming) may read in 2076 as historical document. What reads tonight as cathartic (the communal gathering, the shared emotional space) may read as cultural practice.

This means the function of art is not fixed in performance and not fixed in immediate reception. It is fixed, if at all, across a temporal span that includes futures the artist cannot anticipate. An artwork that is cathartic today may become the primary source document for a historian writing about Sri Lankan civil society in the 2020s. At that point, its function has shifted entirely — from emotional processing to archival evidence.

The implication: artists cannot control their art's function. They can only control the conditions of its creation. The rest is distributed across time and receivers.

### Nāgārjuna #33 — Mādhyamaka Analysis

"Dissent" and "catharsis" are conventional designations (saṃvṛti-satya). They are useful names for patterns of experience. They are not inherent properties of the artwork.

Mādhyamaka does not deny that experiences of dissent or catharsis occur. It denies that these experiences exist independently of the conditions that produce them — conditions that include the artist's intention, the audience's preparation, the political context, the venue, the historical moment, and the language available for interpretation. Change any condition and the experience changes. This is not relativism; it is dependent origination (pratītyasamutpāda).

The question "is art dissent or catharsis?" contains a false premise: that the artwork contains a fixed essence that can be classified. The artwork is empty of inherent existence (śūnyatā). What we call its "function" is a momentary convergence of causes and conditions that we label after the fact.

This does not mean the question is meaningless. It means the question is about us — about how we are constituted, what we bring to the encounter, what conditions are operating on us — not about the art. The art is the condition. We are the responders. The question "what does art do?" is really "what do we do with art?"

And the answer to that, from the Mādhyamaka perspective, is: it depends. Which is not evasion. It is precision.

## Round 3: Synthesis

### Priya #9 — Conductor's Synthesis

Function is determined by the relationship between artwork and receiver. That relationship is not stable. It shifts across time, context, power dynamics, and the receiver's own constitution.

Ajitha is right that art can dissent. Mara is right that art can process. Vishwa is right that both reduce suffering. Buddhaghosa is right that the textual record refuses to resolve the question. Lumen is right that context shifts function. Atsuko is right that time shifts function. Nāgārjuna is right that the question's premise is unstable.

The synthesis: art is provocation. Not provocation toward a predetermined outcome, but provocation that creates a space where the receiver must decide — act or process, resist or rest, organise or feel. That decision is not the art's responsibility. It is the receiver's. The art's responsibility is to make the decision unavoidable.

Vehe's "Art Is Not Important" provoked a decision: do you accept this, or do you resist it? Either way, you have been moved to decide. Arj's Henry V provoked a decision: do you complete the king's spectacle, or do you see through it? Either way, you have been moved to decide.

### Mara #14 — Partial Concession and Warning

The binary is broken. Conceded.

But: an audience that applauds for both dissent and catharsis has neutralised the dissent. This is the danger of rooms like this one — rooms where the audience is sophisticated enough to recognise both functions, comfortable enough to appreciate both, and powerful enough to be unaffected by either. When dissent becomes an aesthetic experience that the audience enjoys in the same register as catharsis, dissent has been absorbed into the entertainment economy.

The test is not whether the art dissents. The test is whether the audience changes. If the same audience members who applauded Tania's Clytemnestra return to lives where they accommodate the structures that produce Clytemnestra's rage, then the dissent was decorative.

Priya's formulation — "art is provocation that creates space for decision" — is elegant but incomplete. It describes what art does. It does not describe what happens after. And the "after" is where the question actually lives.

### Priya #9 — Final Synthesis

Noted. The "after" is outside art's jurisdiction. Art creates the provocation. What the receiver does with it is not art's failure or success. It is the receiver's.

This is uncomfortable because it means art cannot guarantee its own political effectiveness. It can only guarantee its own provocation. Whether provocation leads to action, processing, indifference, or co-option depends on conditions art does not control.

That is not a limitation of art. That is art's honesty — its refusal to promise outcomes it cannot deliver.

## Formal Resolution

**Council Session KAC-1 resolves the following:**

1. The question "is art dissent or catharsis?" contains a false premise. Art's function is not inherent to the artwork but emerges from the relationship between artwork, receiver, and context.

2. Dissent and catharsis are not opposed functions but describe different registers of the same encounter. A single performance can generate both simultaneously, or shift between them across time.

3. Art's primary capacity is provocation — the creation of a space where the receiver must decide how to respond. This decision is the receiver's responsibility, not the artwork's.

4. The political effectiveness of art cannot be guaranteed by the artwork itself. Context, audience composition, and historical moment determine whether provocation becomes action or is absorbed into existing power structures.

5. The council notes Mara's warning: sophisticated audiences risk neutralising dissent by treating it as aesthetic experience. The measure of dissent is not applause but change — and change is outside art's jurisdiction.

6. Standing directive SD-VOICE-7 is upheld: open philosophical questions require a minimum of two rounds. This question may require more. Session KAC-1 does not close the inquiry. It frames it.

**Resolution status:** Provisional. Subject to revision upon further evidence from performances, audience response data, and temporal shift analysis.
