# N3: Greek Tragedy & Agency

**Knowledge Node** · Primary Research Document
**Lead:** Buddhaghosa · **Source:** Performance #4 — Tania S: Aeschylus' *Agamemnon* — Clytemnestra's Triumph
**Date:** 8 April 2026 · Colombo, Sri Lanka

---

## 1. The Performance

Tania S delivered Clytemnestra's return from Aeschylus' *Agamemnon* not as madness, not as pathology, but as architecture. The murder of Agamemnon was staged as a calculated, public, political act. Clytemnestra stood over her husband's body and *explained* herself to the chorus — not apologised, not wept, but *ruled*. She assumed the throne. She took command of the narrative.

The force of this performance lay in its refusal of the available categories. Clytemnestra was not a wronged woman driven to hysteria — the familiar portrait of feminine vengeance. She was a politician. She had waited ten years, plotted with precision, and executed. She then faced the chorus of Argive elders and made her case. In Aeschylus' text, she wins that argument. The chorus is silenced. The city, briefly, is hers.

What Tania S made legible is the play's own logic: Clytemnestra is *not wrong by the terms the play itself sets*. Agamemnon sacrificed their daughter Iphigenia at Aulis to secure favourable winds for Troy. He chose military ambition over his child's life. Clytemnestra's response is disproportionate by any ethical calculus — she kills him with an axe in a bath, then murders his Trojan concubine Cassandra — but Aeschylus structures the drama so that her reasoning is *comprehensible*. The audience is forced to sit with that comprehension.

The room in Colombo sat with it. That discomfort was the event.

## 2. Philosophical Lineage

Aeschylus composed the *Oresteia* in 458 BCE. The trilogy tracks the House of Atreus from generational curse through to the founding of the Athenian court system — from blood vengeance to civic justice. But the first play, *Agamemnon*, offers no resolution. It ends with Clytemnestra in power and the chorus unable to refute her.

Martha Nussbaum's *The Fragility of Goodness* (1986) reads Greek tragedy as philosophy — a form of thinking about human vulnerability that analytical philosophy's preference for rational agents systematically excludes. Nussbaum argues that Aeschylus presents characters whose goodness is inseparable from their exposure to circumstance. Clytemnestra is not a villain in the philosophical sense; she is a response to conditions. Her agency is real, and its exercise is devastating. That is the tragic point: agency and destruction cohabit.

Anne Carson's *Grief Lessons* (2006) translates and annotates Greek tragedy with the sensibility of a contemporary poet. Carson emphasises what she calls the "stupidity" of Greek heroes — their refusal to stop, to calculate, to yield. Clytemnestra is the exception: she is the most intelligent character in the *Agamemnon*, and that intelligence is precisely what makes her dangerous. Carson's framing sharpens the question: does tragedy punish intelligence in women?

Simon Goldhill's *Reading Greek Tragedy* (1986) demonstrated that these plays were never intended as timeless moral fables. They were civic performances embedded in specific Athenian democratic rituals. The chorus represents the *polis* — the citizen body. When Clytemnestra silences the chorus, Aeschylus dramatises a woman overpowering the state. That this was staged in a radical democracy that excluded women from public life is not incidental. It is the provocation.

Nietzsche's *The Birth of Tragedy* (1872) remains relevant for its claim that Greek tragedy holds the Dionysian — chaos, bodily experience, the refusal of rational order — against the Apollonian — form, individuation, control. Clytemnestra is pure Dionysian force breaking through the Apollonian structure of the oikos (household) and the *polis*. Nietzsche would call her necessary.

## 3. Sri Lankan Context

Sri Lankan audiences recognised something. The figure of a woman who seizes power, who occupies public space with ambition, who refuses the grammar of modesty — this is not ancient Greece. This is here.

Sunethra Bandaranaike, Sirimavo Bandaranaike, Chandrika Kumaratunga: Sri Lanka has produced women in executive power, but the cultural vocabulary for understanding them remains impoverished. They are permitted to inherit, to mourn, to manage. They are not permitted to *want*. When a woman's political ambition is acknowledged, the language shifts — she becomes "ruthless," "calculating," "unfeminine." The same words used for Clytemnestra.

Kumari Jayawardena's *Feminism and Nationalism in the Third World* (1986) tracks how anti-colonial movements in Sri Lanka and elsewhere produced a "respectable" femininity — women as symbols of national purity, domestic virtue, cultural authenticity. Women who fell outside this script — who were angry, ambitious, sexual, or simply loud — were not merely disagreed with. They were made monstrous.

Tania S's Clytemnestra named this mechanism. The audience saw it.

## 4. Counter-Arguments

Two objections demand engagement.

First, Clytemnestra does not merely kill Agamemnon. She murders Cassandra — a Trojan princess, a slave, a woman with no power, who has just survived the destruction of her city. Cassandra's death is not political. It is elimination. If Clytemnestra's case rests on maternal grief and political calculation, Cassandra's murder reveals the limits of that case. Resistance becomes oppression. This is the play's sharpest edge: it will not let Clytemnestra be a hero.

Second, the framework of "agency" itself can be a liberal trap. To celebrate Clytemnestra as an agent is to implicitly accept that violence is a legitimate form of political expression when exercised by the dispossessed. This is not an abstract question in Sri Lanka, where cycles of state violence and insurgent response have structured decades of political life. At what point does the agency of the oppressed replicate the logic of the oppressor?

---

## 5. Key Tensions

- **Proportion and legibility**: Clytemnestra's reasoning is comprehensible. Does tragedy ask us to accept comprehensibility as justification?
- **Gender and monstrosity**: Is the figure of the powerful woman always already a monster, in Aeschylus and in Colombo?
- **The Cassandra problem**: Can a liberation narrative survive the harm it inflicts on other victims?
- **Agency as trap**: Does foregrounding "agency" in reading Clytemnestra let the play — and its patriarchal context — off the hook?
