# N7: Collective Identity
**Knowledge Node** · Primary Research Document
**Lead:** Vishwa · **Source:** Performance #8 — David: "Who the SWRD are we?" (Spoken Word)
**Date:** 8 April 2026

## 1. The Performance

David's piece opens with the name "S.W.R.D." turned into a profanity — not merely a swear word, but a profanation of the founding rupture itself. He strips the Act of its historical dignity and forces the room to sit with the discomfort of naming what we normally sanitise.

The question "Who are we?" is the backbone. Not asked softly. Demanded. And the answer David refuses to give is the most honest part of the performance: there is no stable "we." Every collective pronoun in Sri Lanka carries a betrayal inside it. "We" always excludes someone. "Our culture" always silences a register. "Our history" always begins after a convenient erasure.

By making SWRD the fulcrum, David places 1956 not as a policy decision but as the moment when the modern Sri Lankan self became a contradiction — simultaneously liberated and imprisoned by language. Sinhala as freedom; Sinhala as cage. The spoken-word form matters here. This is not an academic essay read aloud. This is a body on stage demanding that a room of strangers confront the fraud at the centre of national identity.

## 2. Philosophical Lineage

**Benedict Anderson, *Imagined Communities* (1983):** Anderson argues that nations are "imagined political communities" — imagined because members will never know most of their fellow members, yet in the mind of each lives the image of their communion. This is the theoretical foundation for understanding what David interrogates. If the nation is imagined, it can be reimagined. But Anderson also shows that imagination alone does not make the nation benign — the act of imagining requires drawing boundaries, and boundaries require exclusions.

**Homi Bhabha, *The Location of Culture* (1994):** Bhabha introduces the concept of the "nation and narration" — the nation as a discursive construct that is always in the process of being written, always unfinished. His notion of the "in-between" space is critical here. Sri Lankan identity exists not in a pure Sinhala or pure Tamil register but in the ambivalent, contaminated space between them. Bhabha's work suggests that David's profanation of SWRD is not destructive but productive — it reveals the seams in the national narrative.

**Ernest Renan, *What is a Nation?* (1882):** Renan's famous formulation — that a nation is "a daily plebiscite" — is devastatingly applicable. A nation exists not because of shared blood or language but because its people choose, every day, to continue being a nation. This means choosing what to remember and what to forget. David's piece is an act of refusing to forget what the daily plebiscite requires us to forget: the violence embedded in linguistic nationalism.

**Kwame Anthony Appiah, *The Lies That Bind* (2018):** Appiah argues against identity as destiny — against the claim that our group memberships determine who we are. He distinguishes between identities that are imposed and identities that are chosen. In the Sri Lankan context, ethno-linguistic identity has been imposed with devastating consequences. Appiah's framework allows us to ask: who benefits from insisting that "we" must be defined along the lines drawn in 1956?

## 3. Sri Lankan Context

**S.W.R.D. Bandaranaike and the 1956 Sinhala Only Act:** The Official Language Act of 1956 did not merely change the language of government — it changed the conditions of citizenship. Tamil-speaking Sri Lankans were not only inconvenienced; they were told that the nation did not, in its official self-conception, include them as full participants. This is the rupture David names. It is not ancient history. Its consequences are alive in every ethnically charged political moment, every recruitment poster for chauvinist movements, every "Sinhala Buddhist" sign at a temple gate.

**Martin Wickramasinghe:** Wickramasinghe's Sinhala modernism — particularly in works like *Gammadda* and *Yuganthaya* — offers an alternative vision of Sinhala identity that is rooted in rural life, progressive thought, and openness to modernity rather than reactionary nationalism. He warned, decades before 1956, against using language and culture as weapons. His work is a reminder that Sinhala identity and exclusionary nationalism are not the same thing, even though they have been made to appear so.

**Language as citizenship marker:** The Sri Lankan case is a stark illustration of how language functions as a gatekeeper. The 1956 Act made linguistic competence synonymous with political belonging. The consequences: an educated Tamil middle class shut out of public service, the deepening of ethnic enmity, and ultimately civil war. The question "who are we?" cannot be separated from the question "who gets to speak?"

**Post-war identity politics:** In the aftermath of the civil war, "who are we?" has become even more fraught. The state promotes a singular national identity (Sinhala Buddhist, essentially) while paying lip service to multiculturalism. The diaspora asks the question from outside. The Upcountry Tamil community, the Malay community, the Muslim community — each exists in a different relationship to the dominant "we." David's piece forces the Colombo creative class to recognise that their "we" is the most privileged and the most dishonest.

## 4. Counter-Arguments

Is "who are we?" an elitist question? For most Sri Lankans, identity is not a philosophical puzzle but a practical fact — determined by the name on a birth certificate, the language spoken at home, the postcode. Working-class Sri Lankans do not lie awake pondering the ontological status of the nation. They navigate its consequences. There is a risk that David's piece, and this research document, aestheticises a problem that is, for many people, a matter of survival.

Furthermore, collective identity has genuine functions. It provides solidarity, mutual aid, a sense of belonging. The deconstruction of "we" can serve neoliberal individualism as much as it serves liberation. Not every collective identity is a prison. Some are lifelines.

## 5. Key Tensions

- Imagination vs. imposition: Is national identity something we actively imagine or something done to us?
- Language as liberation vs. language as prison: Can Sinhala be both simultaneously?
- Deconstruction vs. solidarity: Does interrogating "we" weaken the bonds that marginalised communities need?
- The Colombo bubble: Who has the luxury of asking "who are we?" and who is forced to answer by the state?
- Historical amnesia: The daily plebiscite requires forgetting — but forgetting what, and at whose cost?
