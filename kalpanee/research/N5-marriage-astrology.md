# N5: Marriage & Astrology

**Knowledge Node** · Primary Research Document
**Lead:** Vishwa · **Source:** Performance #9 — Tanya W: Marriage Horoscopes (Poetry)
**Date:** 8 April 2026 · Colombo, Sri Lanka

---

## 1. The Performance

Tanya W read poems about horoscopes. Not about the stars but about the people who read them, the families who trade them, the marriages that begin or end over a printed chart. The poems treated *porondam* — the Sinhala practice of astrological compatibility matching — not as superstition but as architecture: a system that shapes who marries whom, who is never considered, and what counts as a legitimate union.

The performance exposed porondam as social technology. The horoscope does not merely describe compatibility; it *produces* it by making certain unions unthinkable before they are ever attempted. Twenty categories of assessment — from *ganaya* (sexual compatibility) to *ayushaya* (longevity) to *chutiya* (caste proximity) — constitute a classification system that encodes social hierarchies as cosmic facts. The stars do not discriminate. The people reading them do.

The poems traced the moments when the chart speaks: the family meeting that goes silent after a low score, the bride who learns she is "astrologically unsuitable" for the man she loves, the couple who marries despite a bad match and must then navigate the social friction of having defied the cosmos. In each case, the horoscope is not consulted — it is obeyed. The question the performance raised, and did not resolve, is whether porondam serves the couple or the family that deployed it.

## 2. Philosophical Lineage

### Pierre Bourdieu — *Distinction* (1979)

Bourdieu's concept of *symbolic violence* describes the way dominant groups impose their categories of judgment on subordinate groups, who then experience those categories as natural and inevitable rather than constructed. Porondam is a textbook case. The astrological criteria that determine marriage eligibility — caste alignment, planetary positions, birth times — are treated as cosmic law when they function as class and ethnic gatekeeping. The woman who accepts an astrological rejection as "just the way things are" has internalised the system that excludes her. The violence is symbolic because it does not require physical force; it operates through consent, through the apparent neutrality of the chart. Bourdieu would read porondam as a mechanism by which families convert social capital into matrimonial outcomes and present those outcomes as fated.

### Gananath Obeyesekere — Astrology in Sri Lankan Religious Practice

Obeyesekere's ethnographic work on Sri Lankan astrology demonstrated that horoscopes function as *narrative technologies* — they provide a shared language for interpreting life events, assigning causality, and managing uncertainty. Astrology in Sri Lanka is not a fringe practice; it is embedded in Buddhist ritual, medical diagnosis, and agricultural planning. Obeyesekere argued that the horoscope operates as a *cognitive map*: it organises experience into a legible structure, reducing the chaos of contingency into a system of correspondences. The relevance to marriage is direct: porondam gives families a rational, shared framework for making decisions that are otherwise driven by emotion, prejudice, and anxiety. Whether this function is emancipatory or oppressive depends on who controls the reading.

### Ulrich Beck — *The Normal Chaos of Love* (1990)

Beck argued that modernity has eroded the institutional foundations of marriage — class, religion, extended family — leaving individuals to negotiate intimacy without the scaffolding that previously structured it. In Beck's framework, porondam represents a *pre-modern residue*: a remnant of arranged marriage logic that persists in a society undergoing rapid individualisation. The tension in contemporary Sri Lanka, as Beck's analysis predicts, is between the desire for autonomous choice ("love marriage") and the need for institutional validation ("arranged marriage"). The hybrid form — what some participants called "arranged love," where families introduce partners but the individuals retain veto — is precisely the negotiated space Beck identified.

### Anthony Giddens — *The Transformation of Intimacy* (1992)

Giddens introduced the concept of the "pure relationship": a relationship entered into for its own sake, maintained only as long as it satisfies both partners, and dissolved when it ceases to do so. Porondam is the antithesis of the pure relationship. It is a relationship entered into for the sake of families, maintained by social obligation, and sustained even when it fails to satisfy the individuals within it. Giddens' framework illuminates why younger Sri Lankans experience porondam as oppressive: not because they reject astrology per se, but because it anchors marriage in kinship logic rather than individual choice. The conflict is not tradition versus modernity but two competing regimes of legitimation.

## 3. Sri Lankan Context

### The Porondam System

The standard Sinhala porondam assessment evaluates twenty categories, though ten are considered critical. *Ganaya* assesses sexual compatibility through nakshatra alignment. *Chutiya* evaluates caste proximity — not in the Indian sense of rigid varna, but as a measure of social distance between families. *Ayushaya* compares the planetary periods governing each partner's longevity, ensuring neither will be widowed prematurely. *Mahendra porondam* examines the fertility prospects of the union. *Vedha porondam* checks for planetary conflicts that could produce illness or misfortune. Each category is scored, and a minimum threshold determines whether the match proceeds.

The system is not uniform across Sri Lanka. Tamil Hindu porondam follows a different calculative method (*porutham*) rooted in Vedic astrology. Muslim communities in Sri Lanka may consult astrologers but do not use porondam as a formal prerequisite. Among Sinhala Buddhists, the practice varies by region, class, and urbanisation. Colombo's middle class is more likely to treat porondam as advisory; rural families treat it as binding. The variation itself is significant: it reveals that porondam is not a fixed tradition but a flexible tool whose rigidity correlates with the social conservatism of the community deploying it.

### Endogamy Through Cosmic Encoding

The most consequential function of porondam is the enforcement of endogamy. When *chutiya* scores low, the families are told the match is cosmically inadvisable. The actual objection is usually social — class difference, ethnic difference, regional difference — but the horoscope provides a respectable, non-confrontational language for refusal. The rejected party cannot argue with the stars. The family cannot be accused of prejudice. The cosmos absorbs the social conflict and neutralises it. This is Bourdieu's symbolic violence in its most efficient form: discrimination that presents itself as destiny.

### The Marriage Industry

Porondam sustains a significant professional class. Astrologers (*ganitha kendraya*) are consulted at every stage: birth, education, employment, marriage, childbirth, illness, death. The marriage consultation is often the most lucrative. Families pay substantial fees for detailed compatibility reports, remedial rituals for low scores, and auspicious-time calculations for the ceremony itself. The industry is not merely commercial; it is institutional. Marriage proposals in Sri Lankan newspapers and on matchmaking platforms routinely list horoscope details alongside educational qualifications and family background. The horoscope is not optional metadata; it is a required document.

## 4. Counter-Arguments

Porondam is not reducible to social control. Three arguments complicate the critique.

First, porondam functions as a commitment mechanism. In a society where divorce carries significant social stigma — particularly for women — the astrological vetting process provides a form of due diligence. Families are not merely enforcing hierarchy; they are attempting to reduce the risk of marital failure. Whether the system actually achieves this is empirically debatable, but the intention is not purely malign.

Second, porondam constitutes communal knowledge. The astrological tradition represents centuries of accumulated observation about human compatibility, family dynamics, and social cohesion — encoded in a framework that makes it transmissible across generations. To dismiss it entirely as superstition is to discard a knowledge system without understanding what it knows.

Third, the alternatives are not obviously superior. The "pure relationship" model that Giddens celebrates has its own pathologies: serial monogamy, emotional precarity, the privatisation of relationship failure. arranged marriages with porondam vetting have lower divorce rates than love marriages in comparable societies. Whether this indicates success or coercion depends on what you value more — individual satisfaction or institutional stability.

## 5. Key Tensions

- **Cosmic determinism versus social control**: Is the horoscope describing reality or constructing it? Can the two be separated, or does the act of description itself constitute construction?
- **Who benefits from porondam?**: The system disproportionately serves the interests of families — specifically parents, specifically the family with more social capital. The individuals being matched may bear the costs of compatibility decisions made on their behalf.
- **Tradition as living practice versus tradition as museum piece**: When young Sri Lankans invoke porondam, are they maintaining a living tradition or performing tradition for the benefit of older generations? Does the distinction matter?
- **The secular critique problem**: To reject porondam on rationalist grounds is to dismiss a practice that provides genuine comfort, structure, and meaning to millions. Is it possible to critique the oppressive functions of astrology without disrespecting the people who rely on it?
