# N1: Art vs. Importance
**Knowledge Node** · Primary Research Document
**Lead:** Ajitha · **Source:** Performance #1 — Vehe: "Art Is Not Important"
**Date:** 8 April 2026

---

## 1. The Provocation

To declare art unimportant from within an artwork is not a negation but a paradox. Vehe's piece performs its own thesis: if art truly did not matter, there would be no performance, no audience, no statement to make. The act of standing before a room and insisting on art's irrelevance *is* the proof of its relevance. This is not contradiction for its own sake. It is a genuine philosophical move — one with a deep lineage.

The provocation works because it forces the audience into an uncomfortable position. You are watching art that tells you art does not matter. You are moved by a piece that says being moved is beside the point. The discomfort is productive: it separates the *experience* of art from the *claims* made about it, and asks whether the former can survive without the latter.

This is not nihilism. It is closer to Wittgenstein's ladder — a structure you climb and then kick away. Vehe's performance builds a case for art's irrelevance using the very faculties that art cultivates: attention, imagination, aesthetic judgment. The piece is its own refutation, and that is precisely what makes it interesting.

## 2. Philosophical Lineage

### Oscar Wilde — *The Picture of Dorian Gray*, Preface (1890)

"All art is quite useless." Wilde's provocation was not cynicism but defence. Uselessness, for Wilde, was art's highest virtue. Useful art is instrumental — it serves a purpose outside itself, whether moral instruction, political persuasion, or commercial sale. Useless art exists for its own sake. It demands nothing from you except attention. Wilde inverted Victorian moralism by arguing that the moment art becomes useful, it ceases to be art and becomes propaganda, decoration, or advertisement. The preface is a manifesto for aesthetic autonomy: art's value resides precisely in its refusal to justify itself.

### Immanuel Kant — *Critique of Judgment* (1790)

Kant's concept of "purposiveness without purpose" is the philosophical foundation of art-for-art's-sake. When we experience the beautiful, we perceive an object as if it were designed for our judgment, yet we cannot identify any actual purpose it serves. This creates a peculiar pleasure: the mind oscillates between understanding and imagination without settling on either. Art, in Kant's framework, is pleasurable precisely because it resists being put to use. It engages our faculties without satisfying any practical need. This is not a limitation of art but its defining characteristic.

### Theodor Adorno — *Aesthetic Theory* (1970)

Adorno sharpens Wilde and Kant into a political weapon. For Adorno, art's autonomy — its refusal to be useful — is what allows it to resist the "culture industry," the system that turns every human experience into a commodity. If art becomes instrumental, it becomes susceptible to co-optation by power. Adorno argues that genuine art must maintain a critical distance from society, even (especially) when that society demands relevance. The autonomous artwork is not escapist; it is a form of negative dialectics, a way of saying "no" to a world that reduces everything to function and exchange value. The risk, Adorno acknowledges, is that this position can become self-isolating — art so autonomous it speaks to no one.

### Leo Tolstoy — *What is Art?* (1897)

Tolstoy is the most forceful counter-argument. He dismisses the entire Western tradition of aesthetic autonomy as a corruption. For Tolstoy, art is not about beauty, form, or the pleasure of disinterested contemplation. Art is the transmission of feeling from one person to another. Its function is communicative and moral: it binds communities together by sharing genuine emotional experience. When art becomes the province of elites who value formal experimentation over emotional sincerity, it fails at its essential purpose. Tolstoy would find Wilde's uselessness not liberating but decadent — a symptom of a culture that has lost contact with real human feeling.

### Susan Sontag — *Against Interpretation* (1964)

Sontag takes a different route to the same destination. Her complaint is not that art is too autonomous but that it is too *interpreted*. We have developed a habit of translating artworks into ideas, extracting "content" from form, reducing experience to meaning. Sontag argues for an erotics of art — attention to surface, texture, sensation — over a hermeneutics. "In place of a hermeneutics we need an erotics of art." This aligns with Vehe's provocation: the insistence that art is not important can be read as a refusal to let art be reduced to its importance, to its message, to its utility.

## 3. Sri Lankan Context

### Traditional Arts as Non-Instrumental Practice

Sri Lanka's premodern art forms were not "art" in the Western sense — they were embedded in religious, communal, and agricultural life. The *ves natuma*, the initiation ritual of Kandyan dance, is not a performance for an audience. It is a sacred act of becoming. The dancer does not entertain; they transform. The purpose of the ritual is the ritual itself. Similarly, *kolam* masked theatre, while comic and narrative, operated within a framework of communal health and spiritual protection. These forms were useful, but their usefulness was inseparable from their aesthetic and spiritual character. They resist modern categories of "art for art's sake" and "instrumental art" because they predate that distinction entirely.

### Chandraguptha Thenuwara and Pujita Gunawardena

Thenuwara's *Barrelism* series — paintings of security barrels camouflaged with tropical foliage — resists straightforward categorisation. Is it political art? Abstract art? Institutional critique? Thenuwara refuses the choice. The work is aesthetically compelling *and* politically pointed *and* formally inventive. It does not resolve the tension between autonomy and engagement; it inhabits it. Pujita Gunawardena's multidisciplinary practice — spanning painting, installation, and writing — similarly resists instrumentalisation. Her work does not serve a cause; it complicates the grounds on which causes are staked. Both artists demonstrate that the autonomy-vs-engagement debate is not abstract for Sri Lankan creatives; it is a live question with material consequences.

### State Arts Councils and the Instrumentalisation of Culture

Sri Lanka's state cultural institutions have a long history of treating art as instrument. The Arts Council of Sri Lanka, established in 1952, has functioned variously as patron, gatekeeper, and ideological apparatus. State-sponsored art festivals, cultural pageants, and national literary awards all carry an implicit expectation: that art will serve nation-building, tourism, or diplomatic soft power. The artist who refuses this bargain — who insists, with Wilde, on uselessness — risks marginalisation. In a small market where state patronage is a significant income source, the choice between autonomy and survival is not theoretical.

### Luxury vs. Anti-Extractive Practice

The critique of art-for-art's-sake is that it is a luxury position, available only to those insulated from material need. In a Sri Lankan context, where artists navigate economic crisis, censorship, and political instability, insisting on art's uselessness can appear tone-deaf. But there is a counter-reading: refusing instrumentalisation is itself an anti-extractive practice. When art serves a cause — whether state propaganda, NGO agendas, or market demand — it is extracted from the artist and repurposed. The insistence on uselessness can be read as a refusal to be extracted. The question is whether this refusal is available to all artists, or only to those who can afford it.

## 4. Counter-Arguments

Tolstoy's infection theory remains the strongest objection to aesthetic autonomy. If art does not communicate feeling, what does it do? If it serves no communal function, why does it exist? The history of Sri Lankan art — from temple murals to Sarachchandra's stage experiments to contemporary protest art — suggests that the most resonant works are those that *do* something: challenge power, mourn loss, imagine alternatives. The purely autonomous artwork, untethered from any social function, risks irrelevance.

Marxist aesthetics offers another objection. Art is part of the superstructure — it reflects and reinforces the economic base. The claim that art can exist outside ideology is itself ideological, a mask that conceals art's embeddedness in class relations. For the Sri Lankan creative navigating a post-war, economically fragile society, the question "what is this art for?" is not hostile; it is urgent.

The elitism critique targets art-for-art's-sake as inherently exclusionary. Wilde and Adorno wrote from positions of relative privilege. Their insistence on autonomy presupposes an audience with the leisure and education to appreciate disinterested beauty. In a context where the majority lack access to basic resources, the celebration of uselessness can read as contempt for necessity.

## 5. Key Tensions

1. **Can Sri Lankan artists afford the Wilde-Adorno position?** When material survival demands instrumentalisation — art for NGOs, art for the state, art for the market — is autonomy a meaningful option or a first-world abstraction?

2. **Is the ritual-modern distinction real?** Premodern Sri Lankan art forms were both sacredly autonomous (the *ves natuma* serves no external purpose) and socially embedded (they sustained community cohesion). Does the Western either/or — autonomous or instrumental — misdescribe our own traditions?

3. **When does anti-extraction become self-isolation?** If refusing instrumentalisation protects the artist from co-optation, it also risks cutting them off from the very audiences who might benefit from their work. How do Sri Lankan creatives navigate this?

4. **What does "important" actually mean?** Vehe's provocation depends on the word. Important to whom? For what? The question collapses until we specify: important to the nation? to the market? to the artist's own survival? to the audience's emotional life? Different answers lead to different politics.
