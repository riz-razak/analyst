# N9: The Break as Liminal Space
**Knowledge Node** · Primary Research Document
**Lead:** Nāgārjuna · **Source:** Structural — Scheduled break between performance sets
**Date:** 8 April 2026

## 1. The Performance

There is no performer during the break. And yet the break performs.

At Kalpanee's Art Circle tonight, the scheduled interval between performance sets becomes the most socially charged moment of the evening. The room shifts. Bodies that were oriented toward the stage reorient toward each other. Strangers who sat in silence during performances begin conversations — halting, hesitant, then increasingly animated. The hierarchies of performer/audience dissolve. The balcony fills with people who need air, cigarettes, distance from the intensity of what just happened inside.

The break is not empty time. It is the moment when the audience begins to process — not individually, but collectively. The conversations in the break are not small talk. They are the first draft of meaning-making. Someone says, "Did you feel that?" Someone else says, "I didn't understand the second piece." These exchanges are not incidental. They are the event's nervous system, conducting signals between individual experiences and building something that no single person could have constructed alone.

This document argues that the break should be taken seriously as a site of philosophical and social significance — not a pause between performances but a performance in its own right.

## 2. Philosophical Lineage

**Arnold van Gennep, *Les Rites de Passage* (1909):** Van Gennep identified a three-stage structure in rites of passage: separation (leaving the old social state), limen (the threshold — a state of betwixt-and-between), and incorporation (entering the new social state). The break at tonight's event maps onto this structure with surprising precision. The audience has been "separated" from ordinary social life by the intensity of the performances. The break is the limen — the threshold where old identities are loosened and new social configurations become possible. The return to the second set is the "incorporation" — a return, but changed.

**Victor Turner, *The Ritual Process* (1969):** Turner expands van Gennep's concept of liminality into something richer. He argues that the liminal phase is characterised by *communitas* — an unstructured, egalitarian social bond that arises when normal hierarchies are suspended. This is exactly what happens in the break. The performer who commanded the room ten minutes ago is now standing in the same queue for the bar. The established artist and the first-time attendee find themselves in the same unstructured space, with no script to follow. Turner calls this "anti-structure" — a temporary dissolution of the social order that is not chaos but a different kind of order, one based on shared experience rather than fixed roles.

**Victor Turner, *From Ritual to Theatre* (1982):** Turner's later work bridges the gap between ritual and aesthetic performance. He argues that theatre creates a "social drama" — a heightened version of the conflicts and negotiations that constitute everyday social life. The break, in Turner's framework, is the moment when the social drama of the performance spills out of the aesthetic frame and into the real social interactions of the audience. The boundary between performance and life becomes permeable.

**Nāgārjuna and the emptiness of social structures:** Nāgārjuna's *śūnyatā* — the emptiness of all fixed phenomena — can be applied to social structures themselves. The hierarchy between performer and audience is, from a Mādhyamaka perspective, an empty construction: it exists conventionally but has no inherent reality. The break reveals this emptiness. When the lights come up and the stage is vacated, the performer-audience distinction collapses, showing that it was always a provisional arrangement, not a natural fact. The break is not a suspension of reality but a revelation of reality — the reality that social structures are contingent, temporary, and empty.

## 3. Sri Lankan Context

**The pauze in Sri Lankan social life:** Sri Lankan culture has deep structures of in-between time. The tea break is not merely a caffeine delivery system; it is a social institution. Offices, construction sites, homes — the rhythm of Sri Lankan daily life is punctuated by moments of collective pause that are as socially significant as the work they interrupt. The pauze is where hierarchies soften, where information flows through informal channels, where the real work of social bonding happens. The break at tonight's event belongs to this tradition.

**Vesak pandal viewing as communal liminal practice:** During Vesak, entire communities move together through the streets, viewing pandals and lantern displays. This is not merely "going to see decorations." It is a liminal practice — a collective movement through a transformed urban landscape where normal rules of movement, social interaction, and time are suspended. The crowd becomes a temporary community, bound not by pre-existing relationships but by shared presence in a charged space. The balcony at tonight's event functions as a miniature version of this practice.

**Funeral practices as intense liminal space:** Sri Lankan funeral rites — across all communities — are the most elaborate liminal spaces in the culture. They mark the threshold between life and death, between the community with the deceased and the community without. The period of mourning is a structured limen: social roles are suspended, normal activities cease, the community gathers in an anti-structural space. The intensity of the break at an event like tonight's is a distant echo of this depth — a mild form of the same liminal energy.

**The balcony as designed liminal space:** At tonight's venue, the balcony is not an accident of architecture. It is the place where people go when they need to be neither fully inside the performance nor fully outside. It is a physical manifestation of the liminal — a space between spaces. The smokers on the balcony, the people staring at the Colombo skyline, the couple having a quiet argument — each is in a threshold state, betwixt and between the person they were before the performance and the person they will be after.

## 4. Counter-Arguments

There is a danger of overtheorising what is, for many attendees, simply a cigarette break. Victor Turner studied initiation rituals among the Ndembu — profound, life-altering events that marked the transition from childhood to adulthood. Applying his framework to the interval at a Colombo poetry night risks flattening the distinction between genuine rites of passage and the mundane pauses of cultural consumption.

Not every pause is liminal. Sometimes a break is just a break — a physiological need, a moment of boredom, an escape from a performance that didn't land. The communitas of the break can also exclude: the cliques that form, the newcomer standing alone, the social dynamics of the Colombo creative scene reproducing their usual hierarchies in the corridor. Anti-structure can be its own form of structure.

## 5. Key Tensions

- Liminal space vs. empty time: Is the break genuinely transformative or merely a pause?
- Communitas vs. clique formation: Does the break dissolve hierarchies or reproduce them in new forms?
- Designed liminality: Can liminal space be curated, or does curation destroy its authenticity?
- The Colombo creative scene as anti-structure: Is this community genuinely egalitarian, or is the communitas an illusion that masks familiar power dynamics?
- Turner's framework applied too broadly: Is every intermission really a rite of passage?
