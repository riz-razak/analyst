## 1. The Performance

The Chorus in *Henry V* does not merely introduce a play — it issues a contract. "O for a Muse of fire" is not idle longing; it is a request for audience complicity. The speaker admits the theatre's poverty — a "wooden O," a handful of actors, a bare stage — and asks the audience to supply what is missing: armies, kingdoms, the clash of nations. Power in this prologue is not possessed by the king alone. It is co-created every night by a roomful of people who agree, temporarily, to pretend.

This is meta-theatre at its sharpest: the play draws attention to its own insufficiency and then asks you to complete it. Shakespeare makes the audience a co-author of the spectacle. Henry's authority on stage depends entirely on whether the audience consents to imagine it. The wooden O becomes a field at Agincourt only because we say it does.

The question this raises is not abstract. Every performance of power — a political speech, a courtroom proceeding, a military parade — requires the same kind of audience complicity. The prologue exposes the machinery: power needs witnesses, and those witnesses must *participate*. The Chorus is explicit about this transaction. "Piece out our imperfections with your thoughts." Think of those words not as theatrical direction but as a description of how authority actually works.

## 2. Philosophical Lineage

### Stephen Greenblatt — *Shakespearean Negotiations* (1988)

Greenblatt introduces the concept of "social energy" — the force that circulates through narratives, making them resonate across time and community. Power, for Greenblatt, is not a static possession but a dynamic exchange. Shakespeare's plays are sites of negotiation where social energy is produced, stored, and released. The Henry V prologue is a textbook case: the energy of kingship is not inherent in Henry but generated through the collective act of performance. The audience does not passively receive power; it circulates it.

### J.L. Austin — *How to Do Things With Words* (1962)

Austin's theory of performative utterances is foundational. Some sentences do not describe reality — they *create* it. "I pronounce you husband and wife" does not report a marriage; it performs one. "I name this ship" brings the naming into being. The Henry V prologue operates performatively: the Chorus does not describe a kingdom, it calls one into existence through speech. The implication extends far beyond theatre. Political speeches, legal rulings, constitutional declarations — all function performatively. They do not merely reflect power; they enact it.

### Judith Butler — *Gender Trouble* (1990)

Butler extends Austin's insight to identity itself. Gender is not an inner essence expressed outward; it is a repeated performance that produces the illusion of an essential self. The same logic applies to political authority. A king is not born ruling; ruling is something performed repeatedly until it appears natural. Henry's authority in the play is constructed through speech, gesture, costume, and the audience's response — not through divine right. Butler gives us the vocabulary to see power as *reiterated performance* rather than inherent truth.

### Victor Turner — *From Ritual to Theatre* (1982)

Turner draws on Arnold van Gennep's concept of liminality to describe the space between social states. Rituals and theatrical performances create a "liminal space" — a zone where normal rules are suspended and new social realities can be rehearsed. The wooden O is such a space. In it, social hierarchies can be displayed, questioned, or reinforced. Turner's "social drama" framework shows how communities use performance to work through conflicts, redress grievances, and re-integrate after crisis. Theatre is not escape from reality; it is a laboratory for reality.

### Niccolò Machiavelli — *The Prince* (1532)

Machiavelli strips away the mystique of rulership and presents it as technique. The successful prince performs virtue, mercy, and piety because these performances are effective, not because they are sincere. The gap between appearance and reality is not a moral failure but a political requirement. Henry V, as both historical figure and dramatic character, is a Machiavellian performer: the inspiring speech at Harfleur, the feigned mercy after Agincourt, the calculated wooing of Princess Katherine. Machiavelli reminds us that the performance of power is always strategic, and its effectiveness depends on the audience's willingness to believe.

## 3. Sri Lankan Context

### National Narratives Performed into Existence

Sri Lanka's independence in 1948 was not simply a legal transfer of sovereignty — it was performed. The first independence speeches did not describe a nation; they called one into being, much as Austin's performative utterances create realities they appear to describe. Independence Day pageants, with their choreographed military parades and schoolchildren in national dress, are annual rehearsals of the national story. The audience — citizens watching, flags in hand — is asked to complete the performance, to supply what the pageant alone cannot: belief in the nation as a coherent, enduring entity.

### Regi Siriwardena on Language and Power

Siriwardena, one of Sri Lanka's sharpest intellectual voices, wrote extensively on how the English-educated elite performed nationhood while remaining disconnected from the linguistic and cultural realities of the majority. The national project was, in his reading, a performance conducted in the wrong register — English-language rhetoric that failed to reach the Sinhala- and Tamil-speaking publics it claimed to represent. His work is a reminder that performance of power is only effective when the audience shares the language of the performance. When the language gap is too wide, authority appears as theatre — and theatre alone cannot sustain rule.

### Political Theatre and Rhetoric

Sri Lankan political culture is profoundly performative. Election rallies, parliamentary speeches, televised addresses — these are not incidental to governance; they *are* governance. Rhetoric in Sri Lanka does not merely accompany power; it transforms force into authority. The distinction matters: force compels obedience, while authority commands consent. The performative dimension of Sri Lankan politics — the *mahajanaya* (great public) addressed in speeches, the crowds choreographed for state occasions — reveals how deeply the mechanisms Shakespeare describes are embedded in our own political life.

### The 1972 Republic Constitution as Performative Utterance

The 1972 constitution was not just a legal document; it was a speech act. By declaring Sri Lanka a republic, it created the reality it described. It severed the link with the British crown, established a new constitutional identity, and performed the nation's sovereignty into existence. Like Austin's naming of a ship or Butler's repeated performance of gender, the constitution's authority derived from its enactment, not from any pre-existing condition. The question of whether this performative succeeded — whether the audience accepted it — remains one of the central tensions in Sri Lankan political history.

## 4. Counter-Arguments

Performance is not always an instrument of power. Mikhail Bakhtin's concept of the *carnivalesque* describes how performance can subvert, invert, and mock authority. Carnival creates a temporary world turned upside down, where the king is a fool and the fool is a king. This tradition exists in Sri Lankan culture too — in *kolam* masked drama, in satirical street theatre, in the subversive humour of everyday political commentary.

Audience agency matters. The Henry V prologue asks for complicity, but the audience can refuse. They can laugh, disrupt, walk out, or simply not believe. Every authoritarian performance carries this vulnerability: it depends on the audience playing along. In Sri Lanka, moments of public refusal — boycotts, silent protests, creative dissent — demonstrate that the audience retains power even within structures designed to minimise it.

The counter-argument tempers the main thesis usefully: power is performed, yes, but performance is always unstable, always at risk of becoming its opposite.

---

## 5. Key Tensions

1. **When does performance of power become merely performance?** There is a point at which theatrical authority loses its grip on material reality — where the pageant no longer convinces, the speech no longer moves. How do we recognise that threshold in Sri Lankan public life?

2. **Who controls the script?** If power is co-created through performance, then the audience is also a producer of power. But audiences are not unified. Whose imagination counts when we "piece out imperfections with our thoughts"? In a divided society, the question of who gets to author the national narrative is deeply political.

3. **Can subversion be absorbed?** Bakhtin's carnival temporarily inverts hierarchies, but the inversion ends. Can performance that undermines power ever become permanent, or is subversion always re-incorporated by the very structures it challenges?

4. **What is the relationship between sincerity and effectiveness?** Machiavelli assumes the successful performance is the insincere one. But some of the most powerful performances — Henry's speeches, independence orations — seem to require genuine belief, at least in the moment. Does performed power need to believe in itself?
