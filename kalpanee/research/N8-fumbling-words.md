# N8: Fumbling Words
**Knowledge Node** · Primary Research Document
**Lead:** Nāgārjuna · **Source:** Performance #10 — Mika: Fumbling Words (Poetry)
**Date:** 8 April 2026

## 1. The Performance

Mika's piece does not begin confidently. It does not build to a climax. It fumbles — deliberately, visibly, and with a discomfort that spreads through the room. The poem is about the inability to say what you mean, and the performance enacts that inability in real time. We hear the corrections, the false starts, the sentences that trail off into silence.

The central claim: the fumble is not a failure of craft. It is proof that consciousness exceeds language. What Mika is trying to express is larger than the words available, and the gap between intention and utterance is not empty — it is the most honest part of the performance. The stumble is the art.

This is a radical inversion of what we normally value in performance poetry. We reward fluency, precision, control. Mika rewards the opposite: the moment where the poet loses control of the line and the audience witnesses the struggle of a mind trying to articulate something that resists articulation.

## 2. Philosophical Lineage

**Ludwig Wittgenstein, *Philosophical Investigations* (1953):** Wittgenstein's later work dismantles the idea that language is a neutral medium for expressing pre-existing thoughts. Instead, meaning is determined by use — by "language games" embedded in "forms of life." To fumble is to step outside the rules of a language game, to reach for a word that doesn't exist within the current form of life. This is not a personal failure but a structural one: the language does not have the move you need.

His earlier *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus* (1921) makes the point even more starkly: "The limits of my language mean the limits of my world." If Wittgenstein is right, then Mika's fumble is not a stumble over words but a collision with the boundary of what can be thought. The fumble is where consciousness meets the wall of its own expressive capacity.

**Jacques Derrida, *Of Grammatology* (1967):** Derrida's concept of *différance* — meaning always deferred, never fully present — gives philosophical weight to Mika's performance. Every word Mika reaches for points to other words, which point to other words, in an endless chain. There is no "transcendental signified" — no final, stable meaning that the fumble is failing to reach. The fumble is not a deviation from successful communication; it is the truth of all communication, usually hidden beneath the illusion of fluency.

**Samuel Beckett, *Worstward Ho* (1983):** Beckett's late prose is the literary precedent for everything Mika is doing. "Fail again. Fail better." This is not a motivational slogan. It is a description of the human condition as seen through language. Beckett's narrator reduces language to its barest fragments — "On. Say on. Be said on. Somehow on. Till nohow on. Said nohow on." — and in that reduction finds something more eloquent than any complete sentence. Mika's fumbles are Beckettian: the eloquence of failure.

**Nāgārjuna, *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā* (2nd c. CE):** The Mādhyamaka tradition, as systematised by Nāgārjuna, holds that all fixed positions are empty (*śūnyatā*). His method — *prasaṅga* (reductio ad absurdum) — shows that any assertion about the nature of reality collapses under its own contradictions. Applied to language: any claim to have "found the right words" is itself an empty construction. The fumble, in Nāgārjuna's framework, is not an accident. It is the cessation of the illusion that words can fix meaning. *Apoha* (the theory of exclusion) further supports this: words do not positively denote things; they only exclude what a thing is not. Every word is a negative act, a fumble toward something it cannot name.

## 3. Sri Lankan Context

**Multilingual reality as daily fumble:** Sri Lanka's linguistic landscape — Sinhala, Tamil, English, and the many registers within each — means that code-switching is not a choice but a necessity. Most educated Sri Lankans inhabit a space between languages where complete fluency in any single one is rare. The daily speech of a Colombo creative is often a fumble: a sentence that starts in English, switches to Sinhala for emphasis, borrows a Tamil word, and ends in a register that belongs to none of the above. Mika's performed fumble mirrors this lived reality. The gap between intention and expression is not philosophical abstraction here; it is the texture of daily communication.

**Poetry in translation:** Sri Lankan poetry exists across languages, and translation is always an act of fumbling. What survives a translation from Sinhala to English is never the whole poem. Rhythm, connotation, cultural resonance — these are lost or transformed. The translator is always in the position of Mika on stage: reaching for the right word and finding only approximations.

**Classical vs. spoken language:** The gap between classical Sinhala (or Pali) and the spoken vernacular is a version of the fumble Mika describes. Classical forms carry authority and beauty but are inaccessible to most contemporary speakers. The spoken vernacular is accessible but feels inadequate for certain registers of experience. The poet who tries to bridge this gap is always fumbling.

## 4. Counter-Arguments

There is a risk of romanticising failure. Sometimes a stumble is just a stumble — a lack of preparation, a moment of nerves, an underrehearsed performance. Not every linguistic failure contains philosophical truth. Fluency can be honest. A poet who finds the right word has not betrayed consciousness; they have served it.

Furthermore, the celebration of fumbling can become an excuse for laziness. If the fumble is always already the art, then craft ceases to matter. The audience deserves the poet's best effort at precision, not a performance of helplessness. Beckett, after all, was ruthlessly precise in his failures. His fumbles were the product of extraordinary discipline, not the absence of it.

## 5. Key Tensions

- Fumble as truth vs. fumble as failure: When is linguistic struggle honest and when is it incompetent?
- Consciousness vs. language: Does consciousness actually exceed language, or is consciousness itself linguistic?
- Code-switching as creativity vs. code-switching as colonised habit: Is Sri Lanka's multilingual fumble a resource or a wound?
- Precision as violence vs. precision as care: Does finding the "right word" narrow experience rather than express it?
- Discipline of failure: Beckett's controlled disintegration vs. genuine loss of control
