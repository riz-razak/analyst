# N4: Female Stereotypes

**Knowledge Node** · Primary Research Document
**Lead:** Lumen · **Source:** Performance #5 — Tanya W: Female Stereotypes (Spoken Word)
**Date:** 8 April 2026 · Colombo, Sri Lanka

---

## 1. The Performance

Tanya W stood alone with a microphone and listed the things a woman is supposed to be. The list was long. It was familiar. Every woman in the room recognised it. The power of the performance was not in revelation but in accumulation — each stereotype named, held up, examined, and not quite put down.

Spoken word operates as enactment. Tanya W was not *describing* the double bind; she was *performing* it. She occupied the position of the stereotyped woman and the woman who refuses the stereotype simultaneously. The audience watched her become the object of the gaze and the subject who names it. This is the formal achievement of the piece: the performer is both the one who is looked at and the one who speaks.

The stereotypes themselves were drawn from lived Sri Lankan experience — the good daughter who does not raise her voice, the good wife who manages the household without complaint, the good mother who exists in relation to her children and never in relation to herself. But Tanya W also named the newer, seemingly progressive stereotypes: the "strong woman" who is expected to absorb all difficulty without breaking, the "modern woman" who must be tradwife and CEO in the same breath.

The performance asked a question it did not answer: what does a woman become when she can see the stereotype but cannot step outside it?

## 2. Philosophical Lineage

Judith Butler's *Gender Trouble* (1990) remains the foundational text for understanding gender as performance. Butler argues that gender is not an expression of an inner identity but a repeated set of acts that *produce* the illusion of a stable self. The stereotype, on this reading, is not a false belief about women but a regulatory script that women are compelled to perform — and that compulsion is what makes gender real. Tanya W's spoken word exposed the script by reciting it. The performance was Butler's theory made visceral: to name the performance is to begin — but only begin — to destabilise it.

Simone de Beauvoir's *The Second Sex* (1949) preceded Butler by four decades and arrived at a compatible conclusion through existentialist philosophy. Beauvoir's famous formulation — "One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman" — insists that femininity is a construction, not a destiny. But Beauvoir also analysed the specific mechanisms of that construction: how girlhood is shaped by social expectation, how women learn to experience themselves as objects rather than subjects. The stereotypes Tanya W listed are the concrete instantiations of what Beauvoir called *immanence* — the condition of being defined by others rather than defining oneself.

Gregory Bateson's *Steps to an Ecology of Mind* (1972) introduced the concept of the "double bind" — a situation in which a person receives contradictory messages and is punished for responding to either. Bateson developed this framework to understand schizophrenia, but it has become indispensable for feminist theory. The double bind of femininity is structural: be desirable but not sexual, be ambitious but not aggressive, be strong but not threatening, be independent but not alone. Tanya W's performance mapped this terrain with precision.

bell hooks' *Feminist Theory: From Margin to Center* (1984) pushed feminist analysis beyond the concerns of white, middle-class women. hooks insisted that stereotypes are not merely inaccurate representations but instruments of domination — they organise material reality, distributing power along axes of race, class, and gender simultaneously. For a Sri Lankan woman, the stereotypes Tanya W named are not just ideas. They determine employment, marriage prospects, physical safety, and access to public space.

## 3. Sri Lankan Context

Kumari Jayawardena's *Feminism and Nationalism in the Third World* (1986) is essential reading here. Jayawardena documented how colonial and post-colonial Sri Lankan modernity constructed a particular figure of the "Sri Lankan woman" — educated enough to participate in reform movements but domestic enough to symbolise cultural authenticity. This figure was not a natural category but a political project. The good daughter/wife/mother script is not traditional; it was *invented* by a specific class at a specific historical moment, then universalised as cultural heritage.

The stereotypes persist. In contemporary Sri Lankan workplaces, women navigate microaggressions that reinforce these scripts — being asked about marriage plans in job interviews, being passed over for leadership roles on the assumption that family will take priority, being told to "smile more" in professional settings. Sinhala and Tamil both carry deeply gendered grammatical structures that position women as passive, decorative, or subordinate. The language does not merely reflect bias; it produces it.

Tanya W's performance was understood immediately because the audience lives inside these structures. The question is whether naming them constitutes resistance or merely catalogues the damage.

---

## 4. Counter-Arguments

Two problems resist easy resolution.

First, does performance always reinforce the category it intends to critique? To stand on a stage and recite the stereotypes of womanhood is to reproduce them in public. The audience hears the words, recognises them, and the recognition itself may consolidate rather than dissolve the stereotype. Butler anticipated this problem: subversive repetition can fail. It can become camp without consequences, irony without agency. The question is whether Tanya W's performance generated genuine destabilisation or merely provided cathartic confirmation of what the audience already knew.

Second, can subversion ever fully escape the category it operates within? To refuse the stereotype of the "good woman" is still to define oneself in relation to it. The "bad woman," the "difficult woman," the "unreasonable woman" — these are not escapes from the stereotype but its mirror images. The frame remains. Beauvoir saw this clearly: women who reject femininity are punished for rejecting it *as women*. The category absorbs all resistance. This is the structural bind, and spoken word — for all its immediacy and power — may not have the tools to dismantle it.

---

## 5. Key Tensions

- **Naming versus dismantling**: Does articulating the stereotype expose it or reinforce it? Is there a threshold of visibility beyond which representation becomes reproduction?
- **The double bind as form**: Can the structure of the double bind be used as an artistic form without reproducing its violence?
- **Catharsis and politics**: If the audience leaves feeling "seen," has anything changed? Or has the performance functioned as a pressure valve that releases urgency without redistributing power?
- **Class and the stereotype**: Whose stereotypes were performed? The "good daughter/wife/mother" script operates differently across Sri Lankan class, ethnic, and religious lines. Can a single spoken word piece hold that complexity?
