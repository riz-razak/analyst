# The Analyst — Financial & Business Plan v1

*A Yan service. This plan is the written companion to the interactive **Media Model Dashboard**
(`analyst-media-model-dashboard.html`); every figure here is a slider in that file, so the founders can
move any assumption live while reading. Tax modelling is illustrative, not advice.*

## 1. Executive summary

The Analyst is an investigative-journalism platform built in Sri Lanka with the ambition to become a
full media company serving the Sri Lankan and the wider South Asian diaspora. The strategic problem is
simple and the plan is organised entirely around it: Sri Lanka's domestic digital-advertising market is
too thin to fund serious journalism, so a business that depends only on a local audience cannot pay a
single full-time analyst, let alone a newsroom. The breakthrough is that advertising and platform
revenue are paid by **viewer location, not creator location** — a Sri Lankan operation that earns the
attention of the diaspora in the United States, United Kingdom, Gulf, Canada and Australia is paid at
those countries' rates, six to ten times the domestic figure. The whole plan is the disciplined pursuit
of that arbitrage without losing the rooted Sri Lankan mission that gives the work its meaning.

We reject two conventional answers. We reject the **paywall and the membership-first model** because the
evidence in this exact market is discouraging: The Examiner, a credible Colombo subscription outlet run
by Daniel and Mimi Alphonsus, has reached only on the order of US$600 in cumulative subscription revenue
after roughly a year while carrying about six staff. Sri Lankans, at scale, do not pay for news. We also
reject the lazy Western wisdom that "advertising is a dead end for publishers"; in a developing market
where Hiru, Ada Derana, Sirasa and Newsfirst all run as free, advertising-funded businesses, advertising
in all its forms — programmatic, YouTube ad-revenue, Facebook Content Monetization, and above all direct
sponsorship — is the realistic engine. Membership is therefore **excluded by design** from the revenue
model, and the dashboard carries no membership line.

The financial conclusion is encouraging at small scale and sober at large scale. A lean,
contributor-led operation can reach the first analyst salary (a Tier-1 wage of roughly US$500/month,
which is essentially tax-free in Sri Lanka) within the first six to twelve months on advertising and
sponsorship alone, with no membership and no paywall. But a fully-salaried newsroom does **not** break
even at Sri Lankan advertising rates at any size; the company is only viable as a **hybrid** — a small
salaried core plus a paid-per-piece contributor tail — which breaks even at roughly ten staff (about
US$5,800/month of revenue) and settles at a thin but real 12–15% operating margin. The model is
load-bearing on two disciplines: diversifying sponsorship so no single advertiser can hold the newsroom
hostage, and scaling verification capacity in lockstep with output so volume never erodes credibility,
which is the company's only durable asset.

## 2. Identity and mission

The Philosophy Council's settled position frames everything below: *scale is neutral; what governs the
volume decides whether it liberates or extracts.* The Analyst's identity statement is therefore not a
marketing line but an operating constraint. We are a liberation-journalism institution rooted in Sri
Lanka's working and aspiring majority, building toward the South Asian diaspora and region. We intend to
fight the attention economy not by retreating from scale but by seizing it for the opposite purpose:
where the incumbents use volume to manufacture outrage and sell agitated audiences to power, we use
volume to manufacture understanding — every piece, however fast or short, carries the context that turns
a scandal into a visible system. Diaspora and regional reach fund and amplify the mission but never
govern or re-centre it. The shorthand the council adopted is: **"news with context, at scale, for the
rooted majority — volume as an instrument of understanding, never of outrage."**

This is not sentiment with no teeth. It is enforced by guardrails that appear throughout the financial
model as hard constraints: a mass-base audience floor (Sri Lankan and Sinhala-engaged service must not
fall below a set ratio even as diaspora revenue grows), a revenue–editorial firewall (advertisers fund;
the rooted base governs), a structural ban on communal bait (the historical failure mode in this market,
where a legitimate grievance is scaled through ethnic heat until the heat becomes the product), and the
rule that we own our distribution spine — site, newsletter, WhatsApp channel — so the rented social feeds
are on-ramps, never the home.

## 3. Market and audience

Sri Lanka is a market of roughly twenty-two million people, mobile-first, Android-dominant, and
data-conscious, with among the lowest advertising rates in the world; Google classifies it in its
lowest tier and local display CPMs sit well under a dollar. A business confined to this audience has a
hard revenue ceiling. The South Asian diaspora, by contrast, numbers over forty-four million people —
roughly eighteen million of Indian origin, seven million Bangladeshi, six million Pakistani, and
substantial Sri Lankan communities — concentrated in exactly the high-value advertising markets: the
United Arab Emirates (about 6.2 million), the United States (6.1 million), Saudi Arabia (6 million), the
United Kingdom (4 million) and Canada (3 million). This audience is English-fluent, news-hungry, often
radicalised by distance, and — critically — monetised at Tier-1 rates wherever it watches.

The audience strategy follows the Strategic Council's segmentation. The rooted base is the Sri Lankan
"awakened frustrated" professional class and, aspirationally, the Sinhala-educated majority; this base is
who we serve and govern for. The growth engine is the diaspora, reached through community-native
distribution — specific WhatsApp groups, diaspora Facebook communities, and country subreddits — rather
than broadcast. The unifying content is the structural frame: debt traps, dynastic politics, crony
capitalism, press-freedom erosion and Gulf-labour exploitation are the *same machine* across South Asia,
and a Bangladeshi in Toronto and a Sri Lankan in Sydney recognise it equally. Pan-regional breadth that
radiates from the Sri Lankan core deepens the mission; breadth that re-centres on the diaspora wallet
betrays it — that is the line the model holds.

## 4. Competitive landscape

The incumbents are instructive precisely because they are both the model to copy and the culture to
reject. A live audit on 19 June 2026 confirmed the scale: **Ada Derana** runs about 3.84 million YouTube
subscribers across roughly 178,000 videos — a churn machine posting forty to fifty items a day, prime-
time bulletins drawing 63,000–70,000 views, and the rest a long tail of clips with low individual
engagement. **Hiru TV** runs about 5.25 million subscribers across only 59,000 videos, with much higher
engagement per post, but its reach is anchored by teledramas (entertainment) rather than news. Both run
every surface simultaneously — broadcast, YouTube, Facebook, owned websites and apps, and increasingly
WhatsApp channels — feeding rented reach into owned assets. The lesson is twofold: the distribution
engine (clip, short, full bulletin, 24/7 live, all cut from one capture) is worth copying exactly; the
rage-bait, decontextualised-outrage and communal-bait culture is worth rejecting exactly. For a *news*
volume benchmark, Ada Derana's churn is the relevant comparable; Hiru's lead is entertainment and is not
a lever we will use.

On the paid side, The Examiner is the cautionary comparable already discussed: a high-credibility,
subscription-only outlet that takes no advertising and finds breakeven genuinely difficult. Our
differentiation is to stay open (no paywall) where they gate, and to add the one clean, capped advertising
layer they refuse — as a supplement, never a dependency.

## 5. Product and content strategy

Three pipelines, deliberately different in cadence and purpose, form the content architecture (and the
output assumptions in the dashboard's Scaling tab). **Statement pieces** — Vice-style, roughly one a
month — are the brand: long-form, designed, video-supported investigations that announce the worldview
and earn citations from journalists and academics. **Big stories** — two to four a month — are the
substance: investigations Hiru would rage-bait in two hundred words, done instead in fifteen hundred with
a network map and sources. **Shorts** — building from a handful a week toward one to three a day — are the
acquisition and virality engine, the answer to "post hundreds and a few go viral," but with a non-
negotiable twist: every short carries a context payload and a source, so volume compounds credibility
rather than burning it. The founder's own throughput — a big dossier in roughly a day when research is
pre-spread — combined with agentic and institutional support, makes a target of two huge investigations,
fifteen to twenty big stories and twenty machine-drafted, human-curated small pieces a month realistic,
**conditional on the verification spine being built first** (Section 10).

The single hardest constraint, surfaced by Mara's gap round, is that machine-drafted volume removes the
human fact-checker who currently protects credibility. Twenty ML-drafted pieces a month is twenty monthly
chances to publish a fabricated number or quote under a named individual — an existential legal risk in
Sri Lanka's ICCPR and defamation environment. The mitigation is the verification spine: a source store, a
claim-binding format where every number and quote traces to a source ID, an Alleged Checker that fails the
build on any orphan, and exception-based human promotion with an audit log — with Sinhala and Tamil never
generated from a language model. This is the precondition for volume, not an afterthought.

## 6. Revenue model (ad-led, no membership)

The model is advertising in all its forms, ordered by realism for this market. **Direct sponsorship and
branded integrations** are the largest and most SL-realistic lever — sponsored segments and "brought to
you by" placements, screened by sector for independence, priced at integration rates far above
programmatic ad-share. **YouTube ad-revenue** is the second engine and the one that scales with the
diaspora arbitrage: long-form English content to a US/UK audience earns on the order of US$4–12 per
thousand views against roughly US$0.70 domestic. **Facebook Content Monetization**, now confirmed live in
Sri Lanka since around December 2025, is a genuine secondary line — in-stream ads on three-minute-plus
video pay roughly US$1–5 per thousand, Reels far less, and only the premium Creator Fast Track bonus
remains US/Canada-only; diaspora viewers lift all of it. **Programmatic display** (one clean ad per page)
is steady but minor at SL traffic levels. Reels and Shorts are reach, not revenue. Grants are a permissible
bridge, never the base.

The "one clean ad per page" question the founders posed has a precise answer in the dashboard's Revenue
Levers tab: a single clean display ad cannot fund a salary alone at Year-1 traffic — programmatically it
would need roughly half a million to a million monthly pageviews — but as part of the stack it is real
money, and a direct sole-sponsor can carry a meaningful share sooner (at the cost of patron concentration,
which the guardrails cap at twenty percent of revenue).

## 7. Financial scenarios and unit economics

The dashboard models four steady-state scenarios; the figures below are the verified council outputs at
prudent rates. The headline is that the operation reaches its first salary on advertising and sponsorship
alone, without membership.

| Scenario | Revenue/mo | Cost/mo | Net | Tier-1 hires fundable |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Lean (months 1–3) | ~$272 | ~$306 | −$34 | founder bootstraps |
| Base (month ~6) | ~$910 | ~$546 | +$363 | ~0.7 |
| Growth (month ~12) | ~$2,508 | ~$1,026 | +$1,482 | ~2.9 |
| Scale (month ~24) | ~$6,879 | ~$2,226 | +$4,653 | ~9.0 |

Sponsorship and YouTube dominate every column; display, Shorts and Facebook are supplements. On tax: a
Tier-1 wage of LKR 150,000/month sits exactly at Sri Lanka's personal-relief threshold and is therefore
effectively APIT-tax-free; the real business target is roughly US$518/month of revenue to pay it (after a
small foreign-exchange spread and platform infrastructure), rising to about US$610 if the 15% foreign-
income tax applies to the platform's forex earnings. Higher tiers are taxed progressively — a Tier-2 wage
nets about LKR 281,500 after tax, a Tier-3 about LKR 414,000. These are illustrative and must be confirmed
with the Inland Revenue Department before any committed figure.

## 8. Scaling and organisation (0 → 50 staff)

The scaling preview (dashboard, Scaling tab) is the heart of the plan for Sunera's review. The council
modelled the company at three cost structures. A **fully-salaried** newsroom never breaks even at current
Sri Lankan advertising rates — margins improve from deeply negative toward roughly −28% as scale spreads
overhead, but never cross zero, because revenue-per-head is capped by audience saturation and SL ad
prices. A **lean, contributor-only** structure breaks even at two or three people but only because labour
is paid per piece and the founders are unpaid. The realistic operating model is the **hybrid**: a
salaried core (editor-in-chief, lead investigators, the verification/legal lead, and a commercial/sales
hire) plus a paid-per-piece contributor tail for the long tail of output.

Under the hybrid model the numbers are: three staff lose about US$490/month; ten staff turn positive at
roughly +US$580/month (about +9% margin); twenty-five staff reach about +US$2,460 (+14%); and fifty staff
about +US$4,680 (+13%). **Breakeven arrives at roughly ten staff, about US$5,800/month of revenue, near
month ten of the ramp** (the dashboard shows the breakeven month and staff live as you move the target and
stress sliders). The steady-state margin for a Sri Lankan ad-led media company is a thin 12–15%; anyone
modelling above twenty percent at scale is mis-reading SL ad economics.

The hiring sequence is revenue-gated and deliberately ordered. The minimum cell is a founder plus one
investigator plus one video producer. The first true hire, gated at about US$1,000/month of sustained
net revenue, is the **verification/legal lead** — because defamation risk, not writer count, is the
binding constraint on everything. Then an editor, then a distribution/social hire, then operations, then
additional investigators toward the ten-person team, and only later a commercial/sponsorship salesperson
(who pays for themselves through commission and de-risks the founder as the single sponsorship
relationship). Each new analyst is a named, public, credible individual with a beat, operating under
shared central services (legal, verification, SEO, distribution) that amortise as the roster grows — which
is why the marginal cost of analyst N+1 falls even though the margin stays thin.

## 9. Platform monetisation adaptation (YouTube vs Facebook)

The monetisation tab of the dashboard captures the operating playbook. YouTube long-form is the revenue
anchor — the only format that pays at media scale to a diaspora audience, monetised through the Partner
Program (1,000 subscribers plus 4,000 watch-hours, or ten million Shorts views) with ads, memberships and
Super Thanks. Facebook Content Monetization is the live secondary line — in-stream ads on long video are
the real earner, with Reels and Stars as supplements, available to SL pages at roughly ten thousand
followers and six hundred thousand watch-minutes. The production discipline is "one capture, five assets":
the horizontal long-form is edited first as the canonical revenue asset; two or three vertical clips serve
YouTube Shorts and Facebook Reels for reach; a three-minute-plus cut serves Facebook in-stream; and the
text plus a key clip feed the owned WhatsApp channel and newsletter. The switch-on order is YouTube first,
Facebook second as bonus, direct sponsorship on owned surfaces as the high-value third, with WhatsApp,
newsletter and the site built from day one so the business is never one algorithm change from zero.

## 10. Operations, build and the platform

Three operational tracks were taken through four standards rounds and two Tech Team reviews this session,
and all are specified to implementation-ready depth but **held for the founder's go before any code or
deploy.**

The **verification spine** is the precondition for volume: source store, claim-binding, an Alleged Checker
as a blocking CI gate, and exception-based human promotion with an append-only audit log, built gate-first
so the safety net exists before the machine-drafting line goes live. The **universal Yan member flow** is
specified on the canonical People model: one Person at `auth.yan.lk` with product memberships and
`analyst.*` rights, sister-brand labels that are metadata only, single sign-on across the Yan family, and
per-product sessions — with no parallel member store. The **admin sign-in failure** has a confirmed root
cause (the callback does not retry the `reauth_required` error and falls to a raw error page) and a Tech-
Team-approved Phase-0 fix: add the retry, return a branded safe failure page, and remove the committed
Supabase fallback keys; the forced-reauth refinement is held until `auth.yan.lk`'s parameter contract is
confirmed. The **platform design system** consolidates four greens and three off-whites into one Bawa-Earth
token layer shared across reader, admin and dashboard, and the **media-model dashboard** itself is specified
to live behind the admin panel as an authenticated view (`#/business-model`, gated by an
`analyst.analytics.view` right), embedded with Plotly loaded once, while the standalone file is kept for
offline presenting. The Tech Team's build sequence puts the sign-in fix and the credential removal first
(a self-contained, high-value shortlist), hardening second, the blocked reauth contract third, and the
design/dashboard work on an independent parallel track.

## 11. Risks and guardrails

Mara's stress testing identifies the fragilities and the model encodes the guardrails as live levers.
**Sponsorship concentration** is the load-bearing risk — sponsorship is the largest revenue line and, in a
small market, tends to come from a handful of clients; losing one can flip the margin negative, so the rule
is no single sponsor above twenty percent of revenue and a discount applied to forecasts until at least six
active sponsors exist. **Verification cost scales linearly** and cannot be amortised away — a hard ratio of
four investigators to one verification lead is enforced, because outrunning verification means either
collapsing margins or exploding legal risk. **Audience overlap** caps revenue-per-head in a single market,
which is why diaspora reach is the release valve. **Foreign-exchange risk** is real and two-sided: revenue
is in dollars while salaries are in rupees, so a strengthening rupee is a silent margin killer (modelled as
a stress slider). **Key-person risk** concentrates in the founder as editor, brand and lead sponsor
relationship, which the commercial hire is designed to de-risk. And **contributor churn** threatens the
per-piece tail that makes the hybrid profitable. Above all sit the ethos guardrails — the firewall, the
mass-base floor, the communal-bait ban, owned distribution, and surplus flowing back to the base — without
which the company would scale into the thing it exists to oppose.

## 12. Roadmap

Phase 1.0 stabilises the baby: submit Google Search Console, restructure the homepage into a South Asia
desk, launch the Shorts pipeline from the existing dossiers, and publish one flag-planting statement
piece. Phase 1.1 feeds the funnel: Shorts to five-plus a week, diaspora-life and geopolitics angles, and
three to five paid-per-piece contributors. Phase 1.2 proves the model: the first salary funded on
advertising and sponsorship, one channel past ten thousand followers, and YouTube long-form toward
monetisation. Beyond breakeven, the network grows from ten toward fifty as revenue and verification
capacity advance in lockstep, YouTube builds toward a 24/7 presence, and traditional television remains a
deliberate someday-trigger — pursued only when digital revenue exceeds digital cost, a repeatable show
format exists, a broadcast-capable legal entity exists, and the engine runs without the founder in every
loop. As the founder put it, if the YouTube channel is genuinely 24/7, the jump to television is only
technical — but it is the output of a proven engine, never the thing built to create one.

## 13. Governance and compliance

The company runs on the Yan council operating model: a standing Owner-12 steers by default, with the full
council and named committees (steering, finance, philosophy, adversarial, tech review) convened by trigger
word, and every output voiced through named agents. Financial figures do not leave the council without
Hasib's sign-off and a cited source; numbers beyond the verified scenarios are marked as proposed and
require verification. Member and identity work rides the central People model with no parallel store, under
Sri Lanka's PDPA — lawful basis captured per product membership, audit logs storing only hashed
identifiers, and one canonical Person record making data-subject rights tractable. Tax treatment is
modelled, not advised, and must be confirmed with the Inland Revenue Department and the compliance lead
before any committed or public figure.

## 14. The ask and immediate decisions

For the founders' decision: confirm the ad-led, no-membership model and the hybrid scaling structure;
approve the Phase-0 sign-in fix and credential removal (subject to the read-only secret check); approve
the South Asia desk and Shorts-from-existing-dossiers as the first build; confirm the verification spine
as the precondition for the machine-drafting line; and set the sponsorship-diversification and
verification-ratio guardrails as hard policy. The dashboard is the live instrument for testing every
assumption in this plan; this document is its narrative. Together they say one thing clearly: a Sri
Lankan, diaspora-funded, advertising-led media company that pays real journalists is achievable on a thin
but real margin — provided it scales verification as fast as it scales volume, and never lets the people
who pay become the people it serves.

## 15. Distribution and go-to-market in depth

Distribution is the make-or-break discipline of this plan, because the diaspora arbitrage only pays if
the diaspora actually watches. The Strategic Council's clear finding is that the diaspora is not one
audience but a federation of rooms, each with its own gatekeepers: country subreddits, British- and
Canadian-Asian Facebook groups, Gulf-worker Telegram and WhatsApp channels, and second-generation
Instagram communities. The unlock is community-native distribution — earning entry into specific rooms
with content that respects each room's particular grief — not broadcasting to an abstract "South Asia."
A single genuinely good piece on Gulf kafala labour, seeded into three Gulf-worker groups, will out-
perform ten generic "South Asia analysis" posts. This is why the regional investigator network matters
beyond reporting: a contributor already trusted inside a community is the credible voice and the
distribution channel at once.

The production cadence is the "one capture, five assets" pipeline running daily. Each story is captured
once, horizontally, with clean audio; the long-form YouTube edit is produced first as the canonical
revenue asset; two or three vertical clips are pulled for YouTube Shorts and Facebook Reels; a three-
minute-plus cut serves Facebook in-stream; and the text plus a key clip feed the owned WhatsApp channel
and newsletter. Burned-in captions are mandatory on verticals because social autoplay is silent, and
titles are re-written per platform — searchable and curiosity-driven on YouTube, emotional and sound-off-
legible on Facebook. Search remains the long compounding asset underneath the social spikes: submitting
Google Search Console (still undone and free) and building evergreen dossiers that rank for high-intent
queries gives the business a traffic floor that does not depend on any single algorithm. The owned
surfaces — site, newsletter, WhatsApp — are the moat: they are the only audience the company truly
controls, and every social asset exists partly to convert rented reach into owned reach.

The positional strength against the incumbents is deliberately *not* speed. The Analyst will never beat
Ada Derana to the breaking tweet and should not try; it wins the explanation six to twenty-four hours
later, when the audience comes looking for what the event actually means. That window — context over
immediacy — is defensible, ownable, and exactly aligned with the editorial standard that protects the
brand.

## 16. Financial detail: the single ad, direct sponsorship, and combined breakeven

The founders' specific questions deserve their specific answers, all of which are live in the dashboard.
On the single clean ad: programmatically, one ad unit per page reaches a Tier-1 salary only at roughly
440,000 to 600,000 pageviews a month — Year-2-or-3 territory for a new Sri Lankan brand — because the
effective RPM of a single clean unit, after fill and ad-blocking, is about US$0.29 on Sri Lankan traffic
and about US$1.76 on diaspora traffic. The ad is therefore real money as part of a stack but cannot be a
salary on its own at Year-1 traffic. Direct-sold sponsorship of that single slot is the faster path: a
brand-safe sole placement commands a premium over auction inventory, and a single aligned sponsor paying
a flat monthly rate can carry a meaningful share of the first salary far sooner — at the cost of patron
concentration, which is exactly why the guardrails cap any single sponsor at twenty percent of revenue,
require sector screening (no politicians, betting, finance, or large contractors), and demand public
disclosure of every advertiser.

Combined, the picture is comfortable rather than heroic. At Base traffic the stack — sponsorship plus
YouTube ad-revenue plus a clean display slot plus Facebook in-stream plus modest tips and grants — clears
the first salary with headroom, and Phase 1 nets roughly positive on a contributor cost base. The deeper
financial point, and the one Sunera should sit with, is the difference between the three cost models: the
lean contributor model is profitable but only at tiny scale with unpaid founders; the fully-salaried
model is never profitable at SL rates; the hybrid is the only structure that both pays real people and
survives, at a 12–15% margin, breaking even around ten staff. The company should therefore deliberately
hold the largest share of throughput on paid-per-piece terms and convert contributors to salary only as
revenue and verification capacity justify it, keeping at least half of output variable so a bad sponsor
month cannot sink the payroll. A cash buffer of at least three months of salaried payroll is a standing
requirement precisely because a single sponsor loss can flip a quarter negative.

## 17. The first ninety days

The first ninety days are about proving the engine with almost no new cost. Weeks one to four stabilise
the existing asset: submit Search Console, restructure the homepage into a South Asia desk (Sri Lanka,
India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Diaspora, Geopolitics — routing and tags on the existing app), stand up the
Shorts pipeline entirely from the five existing dossiers (a backlog of thirty-plus shorts requiring zero
new reporting), and publish one statement piece planting the flag that South Asia is one extraction
machine. In parallel, and gated behind the Tech Team's go, resolve the admin sign-in with the Phase-0
shortlist and remove the committed Supabase credential, so the founders can actually reach the dashboard
and CMS reliably.

Weeks five to ten feed the funnel: lift Shorts to five-plus a week in regionally credible voices,
prioritise the low-legal-risk, high-CPM diaspora-life and geopolitics angles, and recruit the first three
to five paid-per-piece contributors — a Gulf-labour voice, a UK or Canada diaspora voice, and a Shorts
editor. This is also when community-native distribution begins in earnest: earning standing in specific
WhatsApp, Reddit and Facebook rooms rather than spraying links. Weeks eleven to sixteen prove the model:
the first salary funded on advertising and sponsorship, one channel past ten thousand followers, YouTube
long-form begun toward Partner Program monetisation, and — critically — the verification spine's first
two phases built so that the machine-drafting line can be switched on safely rather than recklessly. The
test at ninety days is not virality, which is luck, but throughput and the beginnings of a credible
revenue mix: shorts shipped per week, the first sponsor signed, the YouTube watch-hours accumulating
toward monetisation, and the verification gate operating in warning mode against a known-good corpus.
