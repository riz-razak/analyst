Who Owns the Calendar?

A governance framework for Sri Lanka's national examinations

POL-2026-10 · 22 July 2026 · The Analyst — analyst.rizrazak.com
All factual claims trace to the POL-2026-10 claim register. Claims marked To verify are not in the register and must be confirmed before publication.

1. The problem in one page

Sri Lanka's G.C.E. Advanced Level examination calendar ran approximately five months late for four consecutive cohorts between 2021 and 2025. The sittings tell the story: a February–March 2022 sitting for the 2021 cohort, January–February 2023 for 2022, January 2024 for 2023, November–December 2024, and a November 2025 sitting suspended by Cyclone Ditwah and completed in January 2026. In 2026 the exam returns to its historical August slot — 10 August to 5 September — in a single step. The entire five-year correction is absorbed by one cohort.

The state's public defence of that decision rests on one number. The Ministry states that NIE teacher guides require 300 school days and that 2026 candidates will have had 343 school days to 7 August 2026. The figure may be right. But its derivation has never been published: how 343 was reached within the stated window, how the Ministry's own concession of 15 school days lost to Cyclone Ditwah was netted out, and how the benchmark applies at all — the NIE's 300 days is a syllabus-completion guideline for teachers, not a student-revision standard. A national aggregate, unaudited, is the whole of the case.

Nor was the correction accompanied by mitigation. Every comparable system that restored a post-COVID examination calendar paired the restoration with published, cohort-specific relief: India's CBSE cut the syllabus 30% and restored its calendar in graded steps; Bangladesh published a reduced NCTB short syllabus for its 2021 and 2022 HSC; the United Kingdom made 2022 an explicit transition year with advance topic information, increased question choice, formula sheets and grading pegged at a 2019–2021 midpoint. All three relieved content first and normalised the calendar last. Sri Lanka did the reverse: it restored the calendar in one step and, by Ministry circular of 10 March 2026, made completion of the full syllabus mandatory. No syllabus reduction, no increased question choice and no announced grading adjustment was found to have been offered to the 2026 cohort.

The government's case deserves an honest hearing, and this paper carries it. Somebody must absorb the correction: every year of continued delay conscripts a new cohort into the same position, and the delayed calendar itself costs graduates months of working life. The dates were announced eleven months in advance, on 14 September 2025. The 2025 results were returned in 70 days — the fastest turnaround since 2020, against a prior range of four to six and a half months — which is what makes an August 2026 sitting arithmetically possible at all. And the disrupted cohorts did not visibly underperform: university qualification rates from 2020 to 2025 sit in a narrow 62.6–64.7% band, though that rate is norm-influenced and a weak proxy for learning, so the absence of a drop is not evidence of no harm.

But notice what the strongest version of the government's case still cannot answer: by what process was this decision made, who was consulted, and where is the working? Repeat candidates face four months and ten days between their results and the exam, against 178 and 198 days in the two preceding cycles — a number both sides accept while disputing its sufficiency. In December 2025, the Ministry Secretary cancelled the third-term examinations nationwide "in consideration of children's mental well-being" after Cyclone Ditwah, so the 2026 cohort entered Grade 13 with no end-of-Grade-12 assessment; seven months later the same Ministry declined to move the A/L on materially the same grounds. Whether these decisions were right or wrong, nothing obliged anyone to explain them, consult on them, or publish the arithmetic beneath them. That is the problem this paper addresses.

2. A governance gap, not a scheduling accident

It is tempting to read 2026 as a one-off dispute about dates. It is better read as the predictable output of a statutory design. Examination dates are set by the Commissioner General of Examinations under the Public Examinations Act No. 25 of 1968. The Act vests the power; it prescribes no process. There is no published procedure for how a date is chosen, no statutory consultation requirement, no minimum notice period, no duty to publish reasons, and no transparency obligation attaching to any adequacy claim the state makes in defence of a date. Whether the NIE — which owns syllabus guidance — or the UGC — which owns the z-score and university allocation — is formally consulted before a date is set is not publicly known. Even the instrument by which the 2026 shift to August was decided, and by whom, has not been established from the public record.

A discretion this wide is not unusual for a 1968 statute. What is unusual is that it has survived unexamined through the most disrupted examination decade in the country's history. When the calendar drifted five months, no instrument required a published recovery plan. When it was snapped back in one step, no instrument required an impact assessment, a consultation, or a statement of reasons. When the state defended the decision with a school-day count, no instrument required the methodology to be published — and it was not. The nine students who challenged the timetable in the Court of Appeal were, in effect, asking a court to supply the procedural scrutiny that the statute never built in; and Sri Lankan A/L postponements to date (2020, 2022, 2025) have all been executive and administrative acts, never court-ordered.

Comparable systems structure the equivalent decision differently. The United Kingdom's 2022 arrangements — the advance information, increased optionality and midpoint grading noted above — were published, cohort-specific measures announced in advance, not discretionary acts explained after the fact; the UK regulator Ofqual operates under statutory consultation duties for changes of this kind To verify — confirm the statutory basis, Apprenticeships, Skills, Children and Learning Act 2009, before publication. India's CBSE, similarly, publishes its examination schedules well in advance as a matter of settled administrative practice To verify — confirm typical notice periods from CBSE date-sheet releases. What the register verifies without qualification is the deeper point: when India, Bangladesh and the UK restored their calendars, the restoration was accompanied by published, rule-like mitigation. The decision was visible. In Sri Lanka it is not, because nothing requires it to be.

This is why no repair confined to 2026 is adequate. The 2026 cohort's position is now largely fixed. The governance gap is not.

3. The framework

Five proposals follow. Each names the deciding institution and the instrument. They are deliberately modest: none second-guesses any particular date, and none transfers the scheduling power away from the Department of Examinations. They discipline how the power is exercised, not who holds it.

3.1 A statutory multi-year examination calendar

Parliament should amend the Public Examinations Act to require the Commissioner General to publish a rolling multi-year calendar for the G.C.E. O/L and A/L, with each sitting's window fixed and published at least 24 months ahead. The calendar would be amendable only through a defined force-majeure procedure — a documented determination by the Commissioner General, confirmed by the Minister, with reasons published within a fixed period. The 2026 date was announced eleven months out, which is better notice than critics have generally credited; the point of a statutory 24-month horizon is that adequate notice should not depend on grace. A published forward calendar also converts any future drift into a visible, accountable event: a sitting cannot quietly slip five months if the statute requires the slippage to be declared and reasoned.

Deciding institution: Parliament. Instrument: amendment to the Public Examinations Act No. 25 of 1968.

3.2 A transparency duty on adequacy claims

Where the Department or Ministry asserts, in defence of a scheduling decision, that preparation time is adequate — a school-day count, a syllabus-coverage figure, or equivalent — the full methodology must be published simultaneously: the counting window, the counting rules, the treatment of closures and disruptions, and the benchmark against which the count is measured, including that benchmark's intended purpose. Had this duty existed in 2026, the 343-day figure would have been published with its derivation and with the NIE 300-day guideline it invokes, and the central public dispute of the year — which was definitional before it was factual, since the state's two-year teaching-day total and the students' elapsed-preparation figures measure different quantities — could have been resolved on paper rather than litigated. This duty needs no legislation to begin: it is a publication practice the Ministry Secretary can adopt by circular tomorrow, and entrench in the Act later.

Deciding institution: Ministry of Education (immediately, by circular); Parliament (entrenchment). Instrument: Ministry circular; subsequently a statutory duty in the amended Act.

3.3 A mitigation protocol for calendar compression

Where a calendar change compresses a cohort's preparation interval beyond a defined threshold — measured against the preceding cycle and against the published multi-year calendar — a mitigation review must be triggered automatically. The reviewing body would choose from a published menu drawn from verified international practice: advance notice of examinable topics, increased question choice, and standards protection in grading (for Sri Lanka, an announced z-score baseline commitment — technically straightforward, since the z-score is already norm-referenced). The decision would be taken jointly by the Department of Examinations, the NIE and the UGC, with the outcome published, including a reasoned decision to apply no mitigation if that is the conclusion. The point is not that every compression demands relief; it is that the question must be formally asked, jointly answered, and answered in public. In 2026 there is no evidence the question was ever formally put.

Deciding institution: DoE, NIE and UGC jointly. Instrument: a standing tri-institutional protocol adopted by circular, referenced in the amended Act.

3.4 A cohort-impact assessment before date changes

Before any change to a published examination date, the Department must complete and publish a cohort-impact assessment identifying every affected candidate class — school candidates, repeat candidates, and private candidates — and the change's effect on each group's preparation interval. The 2026 episode shows why the enumeration matters: repeat candidates, whose interval fell to four months and ten days from 178–198 days in prior cycles, are the group with the shortest runway, and even their number within the 2026 candidature is not publicly established. Groups invisible in the data are invisible in the decision. The assessment should be short, standardised, and mandatory — closer to a checklist than a treatise — so that it disciplines the decision without becoming a pretext for delay.

Deciding institution: Department of Examinations. Instrument: departmental practice direction, entrenched in the amended Act.

3.5 Sequencing reform: statutory maximum intervals

The examination calendar does not end when scripts are collected. The 2020–2025 record shows results turnarounds ranging from four to six and a half months, collapsed to 70 days in 2026 — proof that the system can move quickly when it chooses to, and grounds to suspect that the earlier intervals reflected the absence of any binding standard rather than any fixed constraint. The amended Act should set statutory maximum intervals from examination to results release, and from results release to the commencement of university admission, with any breach requiring a published explanation. Two cautions. First, the 70-day turnaround was achieved by compressing marking, and marking capacity — examiner numbers, scripts per marker, marking days — is entirely unpublished; a statutory deadline without a published capacity plan risks mandating speed the system cannot sustainably deliver, in a system where teacher unions have previously withdrawn from marking over pay. Second, the interval targets should therefore be set only after the Department publishes its marking-capacity data, which this proposal would also require.

Deciding institution: Parliament (intervals); Department of Examinations and UGC (capacity publication and admission sequencing). Instrument: amended Act; departmental publication.

4. Costing and feasibility

The direct fiscal cost of this framework is close to nil. Nothing in it builds institutions, hires staff, or spends money beyond the administrative cost of publication. Its costs are of a different kind: discretion surrendered, and working exposed to audit. That is the point.

What requires legislation: the statutory multi-year calendar and force-majeure procedure (3.1), entrenchment of the transparency duty (3.2), the statutory maximum intervals (3.5), and statutory anchoring of the mitigation protocol and impact assessment (3.3, 3.4). All of these can travel in a single short amendment Act to the Public Examinations Act — one bill, one parliamentary slot.

What requires only circulars or practice change, available now: the transparency duty can begin with the next adequacy claim the Ministry makes; the cohort-impact assessment is a practice direction the Commissioner General can issue; the tri-institutional mitigation protocol needs a memorandum among DoE, NIE and UGC and a circular; and a forward calendar can simply be published, statute or no statute — the 2027 and 2028 A/L windows could be announced this year.

Before the 2027 cycle, all of the following are achievable without Parliament: publication of the 2027–2029 examination calendar; publication of the 343-day derivation and the NIE guideline as the first act of the new transparency practice; the DoE–NIE–UGC protocol signed and published; and the impact-assessment template adopted. The amendment Act can follow in ordinary time. The legislative environment is arguably favourable: a government can enact this framework as a codification of discipline rather than a confession of error, since nothing in it adjudicates the 2026 decision itself.

One gap in the evidence base should be closed alongside: the instrument by which the 2026 shift to August was decided — who decided, when, and under what authority — has not been established from the public record. An RTI request for that record, and for the 343-day methodology, remains the highest-value, lowest-cost accountability step available to any actor, including the think-tank community. As of this writing, no Sri Lankan policy institution — IPS, Verité Research, Advocata, or LIRNEasia — has published on examination-calendar governance. The space this paper occupies is empty. It should not remain so.
