The Strait of Hormuz is only about 33 km wide at its narrowest. About 20% of the world's oil passes through it every day. When Iran says it's "closed," even a partial disruption sends global prices soaring — and Sri Lanka, at the end of the supply chain, feels it hardest.

Understanding the supply chain
Where does Sri Lanka's fuel actually come from?

Think of Sri Lanka's fuel supply as two streams:

Stream 1 — Crude oil (raw oil that gets refined): Almost all of it (98–99%) comes from the UAE. It arrives at Colombo Port, travels through an aging 5.8-km pipeline to our only refinery at Sapugaskanda (built by Iran in 1969). This refinery can only cover about 30% of our needs — and the pipeline urgently needs replacing.

Stream 2 — Already-refined fuel (petrol, diesel, jet fuel): This is more diversified. Singapore supplies about 27%, UAE 18%, Malaysia 17%, Oman 11%, India 9%, and Saudi Arabia 8%. Some of these routes don't pass through Hormuz.

Cooking gas (LPG) is a separate worry. About 75% has historically come through Hormuz. Litro Gas (which controls 75–85% of the market) receives a new shipment every 3–4 days, and the national storage holds only 6–7 days' supply. A Swiss-sourced US supply contract signed in December 2025 may help, but it hasn't been tested under crisis conditions.

Official messaging vs. the data
What the government says — and what the numbers show

The government's message has been consistent: "Supply is secure. We have 37 days of fuel. India and Singapore will bridge any gap." This is technically accurate in the very short term. But it systematically understates the full picture.

Yes — 37 days. But this is commercial inventory, not a strategic reserve. It's simply what's in the system for daily operations. IEA countries maintain 90 days. The Trincomalee Oil Tank Farm could store months of reserves (1.2 million tonnes), but after decades of neglect, only 15 of 99 tanks are operational. Successive governments have failed to develop it.

India and Singapore do supply refined products. But they'll face their own supply pressures if Hormuz stays closed — India imports 85% of its crude from the Gulf. And the issue isn't just getting the fuel — it's the price. If Brent moves from $72 to $140/barrel, that adds $2.9 billion to Sri Lanka's annual bill — equivalent to 43% of total forex reserves.

The starting position is indeed better than 2022 (reserves were near-zero then). But the double vulnerability is identical: energy costs surge while Gulf remittances ($8B+ from 1.5 million workers) face collapse. In 2022, this combination caused GDP to contract 7.8%, fuel rationing, 10–15 hour power cuts, and deaths in fuel queues. The government has no published national energy security strategy.

As of 2023, about 50% of electricity came from low-carbon sources. Reaching 70% by 2030 faces "considerable challenges" per experts. Meanwhile, Sri Lanka's debt-to-GDP ratio of 96–104% limits investment. A nationwide blackout in February 2025, caused by a single substation failure, exposed how fragile the grid remains.

Civil society
What communities can do — the real strength

Sri Lanka's greatest asset isn't its fuel reserves — it's its people. The 2022 crisis proved that communities can self-organise with remarkable effectiveness when institutions fail. Here's what worked then and what can work now:

Neighbourhood fuel-sharing networks — In 2022, communities organised shared transport rotas. One vehicle, multiple families. This reduces individual fuel consumption by 60–80%.

Community kitchens — Cooking collectively is far more fuel-efficient than individual households. Religious institutions (kovils, temples, mosques, churches) can serve as hubs, just as they did during COVID and the 2022 crisis.

Information verification networks — Designate trusted community members to verify and share accurate information, reducing the spread of panic-inducing rumours.

Skills exchange — Those who know how to cook with firewood, preserve food without refrigeration, or maintain solar equipment can teach others. Knowledge is the one resource that multiplies when shared.

Internal politics
The political landscape — what to understand

The Dissanayake government faces an impossible balancing act. The IMF demands cost-recovery pricing (fuel prices that cover actual costs, with no subsidies). But if global oil prices surge, passing the full cost to consumers risks repeating the social unrest of 2022.

An additional complication: the Petroleum Dealers Association has threatened to stop placing fuel orders due to a commission dispute. This is a genuinely complex issue that both sides have legitimate claims about — and it's happening at the worst possible time.

What happened: The CPC abolished a 3% commission that fuel station dealers had received since 2019, replacing it with a new formula based on operational costs. This effectively cut dealer margins from ~3% to ~1.7% — a 43% reduction in income. In response, the Petroleum Dealers Association (representing ~1,400 CPC-affiliated fuel stations) stopped placing new orders and cut off credit sales to government institutions, including hospitals.

The dealers' argument (and where it has merit):

CPC fuel station owners face genuinely harsher conditions than their competitors. Unlike Lanka IOC and Sinopec dealers — who receive the same 3% commission and also get credit facilities from their companies — CPC dealers must pay upfront in full before receiving fuel. When fuel prices fluctuate, the loss falls entirely on CPC dealers, while IOC/Sinopec absorb losses for their networks. The same government that removed CPC dealers' 3% commission left it intact for IOC and Sinopec dealers. This unequal treatment is a legitimate grievance — it's effectively penalising your own dealers while allowing competitors to operate on better terms.

The government's argument (and where it has merit):

The National Audit Office and COPE (parliamentary committee) found that the 3% commission resulted in Rs. 35.4 billion in cumulative excess payments to fewer than 1,000 fuel distributors — money that should have gone to the treasury. The Auditor General specifically flagged Rs. 4.3 billion in overpayments in 2022 alone. When fuel prices spiked during the 2022 crisis, the percentage-based commission meant dealers' earnings ballooned while the country was in its worst economic crisis. The CPC Chairman's position: a station selling 15 loads/month (the minimum) still earns Rs. 683,415 under the new formula — not Rs. 62,333 as dealers claim. A station selling 30 loads can net over Rs. 300,000 profit after all costs.

Who are the people behind this? The Petroleum Dealers Association operates as a collective trade body — its specific leadership names aren't prominently published, which is itself notable. The vice-president of the related Sri Lanka Petroleum Private Tank Owners' Association, Kusum Sandanayaka, has been a vocal figure. On the government side, CPC Chairman D.J. Rajakaruna has led the restructuring effort. COPE, chaired by MP Rohitha Abeygunawardena, investigated and found that officials responsible for the original overpayment arrangement had left the country — prompting a referral to the CID.

The uncomfortable question — is this opportunistic?

The timing is hard to ignore. A commission dispute that has been simmering since March 2025 escalated to a full order halt on 28 February 2026 — the exact day military strikes hit Iran. Whether intentional or coincidental, threatening fuel supply disruption during an international energy crisis creates maximum leverage. The government called it an "artificial shortage created by organised groups." The dealers say they simply can't afford to operate. The truth likely contains elements of both.

The moral dimension: There are real small operators among the 1,400 stations who face genuine financial hardship from a 43% income cut. But the collective refusal to supply fuel to government hospitals during a crisis — regardless of the legitimacy of the commission grievance — crosses a line for many Sri Lankans who remember the suffering of 2022 fuel queues. The fact that Rs. 35 billion in excess commissions flowed to fewer than 1,000 distributors while the country was being bailed out by the IMF is also hard to justify.

Bottom line: Both sides have legitimate grievances, but neither side is clean. The dealers have a real case on unequal treatment (CPC vs IOC/Sinopec) and the harsh upfront payment requirement. The government has a real case on the excessive past commissions and the need for reform. But the escalation during a geopolitical crisis hurts only one group — ordinary Sri Lankans already anxious about fuel. This needed to be resolved at the negotiating table, not at the fuel pumps.

The government's track record on energy policy is mixed. Positive steps include the LPG diversification to US sources and initial discussions on Trincomalee development. But politically sensitive electricity pricing reforms have already been partially reversed, and there is still no published national energy security strategy.

What citizens should demand, regardless of political allegiance: a transparent, public energy security plan; honest communication about risks (not just reassurance); accelerated Trincomalee development; and a clear contingency plan for each of the three scenarios outlined above.

Supply chain intelligence
The Singapore channel — tracing the origin of your fuel

When officials say "we don't import directly from the Middle East anymore", they're technically correct — but it obscures a critical reality. Singapore, which supplies ~27% of Sri Lanka's refined fuel, is itself almost entirely dependent on Middle Eastern crude oil.

Here's how the chain actually works:

The CPC tender process: Sri Lanka's fuel procurement runs through competitive tenders managed by a Standing Procurement Committee and a Special Standing Procurement Committee, with final Cabinet approval. In 2025, Singapore-based Vitol Asia won a 6-month contract to supply 92-octane petrol (April–October 2025), beating six other bidders. PetroChina International (Singapore) has also been a regular supplier. All pricing is benchmarked to Singapore Platts — Asia's primary oil pricing mechanism.

What Vitol actually supplies: Vitol Asia, headquartered in Singapore, is one of the world's largest independent energy trading companies. They don't own refineries — they procure refined products from Singapore's mega-refineries (ExxonMobil's 593,000 bpd Jurong facility, Shell's 462,000 bpd Pulau Bukom complex) and ship them to CPC. These refineries process crude oil that is ~90% Middle Eastern in origin — primarily from Saudi Arabia, UAE, and Qatar — all of which transits through the Strait of Hormuz.

The uncomfortable truth: Sri Lanka's "diversified" refined fuel imports from Singapore, India, Malaysia, and South Korea all ultimately trace back to Middle Eastern crude. Singapore processes Gulf crude. Indian refineries (which supply ~9% of SL's refined fuel) import 85% of their crude from the Gulf. Even South Korean refineries are heavily dependent on Middle Eastern feedstock. The diversification is in refining geography, not in crude oil origin.

Key finding
Sri Lanka's refined fuel supply appears diversified across 6+ countries, but the underlying crude oil feeding those refineries is 80–90% Middle Eastern in origin. A sustained Hormuz closure would create a cascading supply crisis — first affecting crude imports directly, then choking the refineries that produce Sri Lanka's "diversified" refined fuel within 4–8 weeks.

Critical analysis
The upstream buffer: how long can Sri Lanka's suppliers keep supplying?

Sri Lanka's 37-day fuel reserve sounds reassuring — but what about the countries that supply the remaining 70% of our refined fuel? If Hormuz stays closed, when do their refineries run out of crude to process?

Upstream supplier buffer: how many days before they stop refining?
🇸🇬 Singapore (27% of SL refined imports) ~11 days buffer
No formal strategic reserve. ~17M barrels Jurong Island storage / ~1.5M bpd refinery throughput. ~90% of feedstock is Middle Eastern crude via Hormuz. Singapore processes crude — it doesn't produce it. Once commercial inventory depletes (~11 days), refineries must throttle or halt. Vitol Asia and other traders cannot source product from refineries that have no crude to process.
🇮🇳 India (9% of SL refined imports) ~60 days buffer
25 days crude stock + 25 days products + 9.5 days SPR (Visakhapatnam, Mangaluru, Padur) = ~60 days. But India itself imports 85% of crude from the Gulf and consumes 5.5M bpd — it will prioritise domestic needs. India has indicated it's exploring alternative sources (Nigeria, US), but any spare capacity will go to India's 1.4 billion people first. Exports to Sri Lanka may be restricted within weeks.
🇰🇷 South Korea (~5% of SL refined imports) 210+ days buffer
100M+ barrels in state reserves + 95M barrels held by private firms = 210+ days of IEA-standard coverage. Fourth-largest reserves among IEA members. South Korea is the most resilient supplier — but it's a minor source for Sri Lanka (~5% of refined imports), and under sustained crisis it too would prioritise domestic consumption.
🇲🇾 Malaysia (17% of SL refined imports) ~40–50 days buffer
Malaysia has domestic crude production (~597K bpd) plus 955K bpd refining capacity. About 35% of refinery feedstock comes from the Middle East — the rest is domestic or regional. This makes Malaysia more resilient than Singapore, but its own exports to Sri Lanka would face price pressure and possible government restrictions under prolonged disruption.
🇦🇪 UAE — direct crude (98% of SL crude imports) 0 days if Hormuz closed
UAE crude for the Sapugaskanda refinery transits directly through Hormuz. A blockade immediately stops supply. The refinery operates on existing inventory (~7–10 days of crude at the facility) before halting. UAE itself is sitting on vast crude reserves but cannot ship if Hormuz is closed — only the 1.5M bpd Abu Dhabi-Fujairah pipeline bypasses Hormuz, and that capacity is reserved for UAE's own use.

Where Sri Lanka sits
Sri Lanka ranks near the bottom of regional preparedness, alongside Pakistan and Bangladesh. But Sri Lanka faces a unique compounding vulnerability that no other country in this comparison shares: simultaneous exposure to oil supply disruption, upstream refinery dependency, Gulf labour market collapse, and IMF programme constraints. War economy analysis
If the war persists: Sri Lanka's economic reckoning

Sri Lanka entered 2026 on the cusp of genuine recovery — 4.2% GDP growth forecast, inflation at 1.6%, forex reserves at $6.7 billion, and an IMF programme on track. A sustained Middle East war systematically unravels every pillar of that recovery.

Five transmission channels — how the crisis hits the economy
1️⃣ Oil import bill explosion
Sri Lanka's 2024 refined petroleum imports: ~$2.5 billion. At current $79/barrel: ~$5.1B annual bill. At $120/barrel (Scenario B): +$2.6B/year. At $150–200 (Scenario C): +$4.5–7.8B/year. For a country with $6.7B in forex reserves, a $5B+ increase is existential. The 2022 crisis was triggered by reserves falling below $2B — a sustained $120+ oil price gets there within 12–18 months even with IMF support.
2️⃣ Forex reserve haemorrhage
Current reserves: $6.7B (3.9 months import cover). Every $10/barrel increase drains ~$650M/year extra. At $120/barrel, reserves could drop below the critical $4B threshold within 6–9 months. The rupee, which stabilised at ~Rs. 295/$ in early 2026, would face intense depreciation pressure — a repeat of the 2022 collapse from Rs. 200 to Rs. 360 is not impossible. A weakening rupee further amplifies the cost of oil imports in a vicious cycle.
3️⃣ Gulf remittance shock ($8B+ at risk)
1.5+ million Sri Lankans work in GCC countries, generating $8B+ in annual remittances — the single largest forex source. A sustained war triggers: hiring freezes → salary reductions → project cancellations → potential mass evacuations (as in the 1990 Gulf War, when ~100,000 Sri Lankans were evacuated from Kuwait/Iraq). Even a 20% decline in remittances = $1.6B/year loss — equivalent to losing the entire tea export industry overnight. A 50% collapse = $4B loss, pushing forex reserves into crisis territory.
4️⃣ Inflation spiral → cost of living crisis
Fuel prices feed into everything: transport costs → food prices → manufacturing → services. The 2022 inflation peak hit 70%. Current 1.6% inflation could surge to 15–25% under Scenario B, and 30–50%+ under Scenario C. The IMF projects 5.4% for 2026 under normal conditions — a sustained war makes even 10% optimistic. Low-income households, which spend 60–70% of income on food and transport, would be devastated.
5️⃣ IMF programme derailment
Sri Lanka's $3B IMF EFF programme requires: fiscal discipline, no fuel subsidies, controlled inflation, and growing reserves. A sustained oil shock creates an impossible dilemma: pass costs to consumers (risking social unrest and political instability) or absorb costs through subsidies (violating IMF conditions and risking programme suspension). If the IMF programme is suspended, Sri Lanka loses access to critical foreign financing just when it needs it most. The December 2025 IMF Rapid Financing Instrument shows the Fund is aware of SL's vulnerability, but patience has limits.

Socio-political analysis
The socio-political calculus: what happens to people and politics

Economics is the mechanism. But it's the human consequences that matter. Sri Lanka's 2022 crisis showed that economic collapse cascades into social breakdown and political upheaval in predictable ways. Here's how a sustained war plays out beyond the spreadsheets.

The three phases of social crisis
PHASE 1 — Anxiety & hoarding (Week 1–3)
This is where Sri Lanka is right now. Panic buying erupts (CPC has already released 5M+ litres). Police ban fuel in containers. Social media amplifies fear — WhatsApp forwards claiming imminent shortages trigger self-fulfilling prophecies. Queues appear at fuel stations even though supply is adequate. The wealthy stockpile; the poor wait in line. Trust in official reassurances is low (the 2022 government claimed "no crisis" until the crisis was total). This phase is manageable if the disruption is short.
PHASE 2 — Rationing & social strain (Month 1–3)
If Scenario B materialises: fuel rationing returns (QR-code systems, plate-number rotation). Transport costs spike — bus fares, school transport, food delivery all increase. Daily-wage workers face income collapse (three-wheeler drivers, delivery riders, farmers who rely on diesel). Hospital fuel supply becomes critical. Schools may close. The informal economy — 60% of Sri Lanka's workforce — bears the heaviest burden. The poor and rural communities face disproportionate impact. Inter-community tensions can flare as scarcity creates competition.
PHASE 3 — Political instability & social rupture (Month 3+)
If Scenario C unfolds: the 2022 Aragalaya (protest movement) template re-emerges. In 2022, fuel queues → hospital deaths from lack of medicine transport → mass protests → the president fled the country. The current AKD/NPP government, elected in September 2024 on a reform mandate, faces an impossible political calculus: protect the IMF programme (and lose popular support) or protect the people with subsidies (and lose the IMF programme). Neither path ends well under a sustained $120+ oil price. History shows: Sri Lankan governments do not survive fuel crises. Three prime ministers fell between 2020–2022.
🏗️ The Gulf workers: 1.5 million people in the firing line

Over 1.5 million Sri Lankans work in Gulf states — primarily in Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait, and Qatar. They work in construction, hospitality, domestic service, and retail. Their remittances ($8B+) exceed the entire tea export industry and tourism combined.

In a sustained war: Gulf construction projects freeze. Hospitality shuts down. Airports face closures (SriLankan Airlines has already cancelled 30+ flights). If the conflict widens to Gulf cities — as the Feb 28 strikes near Dubai and Kuwait airports suggest — mass evacuation becomes necessary.

Historical precedent: During the 1990 Gulf War, ~100,000 Sri Lankans were evacuated from Kuwait and Iraq. A 2026 equivalent involving 500,000+ workers would be the largest repatriation in Sri Lankan history. The returning workers face: no jobs (domestic economy is contracting), lost savings (many leave assets behind), and psychological trauma.

The double hit: When workers return, remittances stop AND domestic unemployment surges simultaneously. This is the most underappreciated risk in the entire crisis — it's not on any government talking point, but it could be more destabilising than the oil price itself.

The core lesson from 2022
The Aragalaya didn't erupt because of fuel shortages alone. It erupted because the government lied — told people everything was fine while quietly running out of reserves. The AKD/NPP government's greatest asset right now is its reform credibility. Its greatest risk is repeating the same communication failure: reassuring when the situation demands honesty. Citizens who are told the truth early can prepare; citizens who are told "don't worry" until it's too late revolt.
The bottom line
Structural fragility behind a thin reassurance

The short-term picture offers genuine comfort. 37 days of fuel. Diversified refined product sources. $6.7 billion in reserves (far better than 2022's near-zero). An immediate collapse is unlikely.

But the structural picture is deeply concerning — and worse than the headline numbers suggest. Nearly all crude from one country through one chokepoint. Days, not months, of storage. One aging refinery with one aging pipeline. And the "diversified" refined supply chain? It traces back to the same Middle Eastern crude — Singapore's refineries have only ~11 days of buffer before they run dry. The effective supply window is 25–30 days, not 37.

The critical insight from this analysis: Sri Lanka's vulnerability is triple-layered. Layer 1: direct crude supply through Hormuz (immediately cut). Layer 2: upstream refineries in Singapore, India, and Malaysia lose their feedstock within 2–6 weeks (cascading supply failure). Layer 3: the $8 billion Gulf remittance lifeline — 1.5 million Sri Lankans working in the conflict zone — faces disruption that no fuel reserve can buffer against.

A sustained war doesn't just raise fuel prices. It simultaneously drains forex reserves, collapses remittances, starves upstream refineries, triggers inflation, strains the IMF programme, and creates the conditions for social and political instability. This is not one crisis — it's five crises hitting simultaneously.

What Sri Lanka needs is not reassurance but honest communication, early rationing planning, diplomatic engagement for alternative supply routes, and citizens who understand the real picture well enough to prepare wisely and support each other. The 2022 crisis proved that Sri Lankans can endure hardship — but only when they're told the truth.
