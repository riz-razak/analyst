üèõÔ∏è The Founders: Who Actually Runs This?

The three co-founders of 1xBet ‚Äî Roman Semiokhin, Dmitry Kazorin, and Sergey Karshkov ‚Äî are Russian nationals who reportedly obtained Cypriot citizenship. Fintelegram and Ukrainian investigative outlets documented that Russian authorities placed them on international wanted lists for operating illegal gambling that generated approximately 63 billion rubles. Karshkov, whose background included heading Russia's cybercrime enforcement unit in Bryansk ‚Äî a posting that would have given him intimate knowledge of how to evade detection ‚Äî died in June 2023 at a Swiss clinic at age 42, under unexplained circumstances.

The network's operating principle is deliberate complexity: Cura√ßao registration (low scrutiny), Cyprus holding companies (EU legal shield), BetB2B technology licences (arm's-length separation), and sub-brands that can be abandoned without touching core infrastructure. Byline Times described 1xBet as "the Wagner Group of sports betting" ‚Äî an apt analogy to a semi-deniable enterprise conducting operations at arm's length from its actual controllers.

02 ‚Äî The Melbet‚ÄìDialog‚ÄìThePapare Axis

How an Illegal Betting Ad Ended Up on Sri Lanka's Biggest Cricket Platform

A telecom giant with ICC broadcast rights. A media platform it owns. And advertisements from an operator linked to a criminal network. The question is not whether the connection exists ‚Äî it's documented. The question is why nothing has been done.

The Triangle

Dialog Axiata PLC is Malaysia's Axiata Group's Sri Lankan subsidiary and the country's largest mobile operator. Dialog simultaneously holds (1) the exclusive ICC broadcast rights for Sri Lanka through January 2028; (2) the official national team sponsorship of Sri Lanka Cricket; and (3) ownership of ThePapare.com ‚Äî Sri Lanka's largest cricket media and streaming platform, which also streams matches via ThePapare TV and Dialog Play.

Melbet (Pelican Entertainment B.V., Cura√ßao #134359), which Ukrainian investigators linked to the 1xBet network via shared IP infrastructure and trademark history, has run advertisements on ThePapare.com. Melbet holds no Sri Lankan licence. Online betting is illegal in Sri Lanka. The Gambling Regulatory Authority Act of 2025 explicitly covers the promotion of unlicensed gambling platforms.

üì° Dialog's Structural Conflict

Dialog's position is arguably the deepest structural conflict of interest in Sri Lankan sports media. As ICC broadcast rights holder, Dialog monetises every Sri Lanka match. As SLC's national team sponsor, Dialog's brand appears on every player's jersey. As ThePapare's owner, Dialog earns advertising revenue from the same audience consuming the sport. And within that advertising inventory ‚Äî a gambling operator whose parent network's founders are on international wanted lists.

Whether Dialog executives were aware of Melbet's regulatory status is unknown ‚Äî the company has made no public statement. What is documentable is the result: a company that profits from cricket's governance structure simultaneously hosting advertising from an entity that undermines it.

‚öñÔ∏è The Regulatory Gap

The Gambling Regulatory Authority of Sri Lanka, established under the 2025 Act, has a mandate to regulate both operators and promoters of unlicensed gambling. However, the Authority is newly established and lacks the investigative capacity and institutional precedent to pursue action against a Cura√ßao-registered operator being advertised on the platform of a listed telecom giant.

The ICC Anti-Corruption Code's provisions on gambling associations apply to individuals (players, officials, administrators), not to media companies or telecom sponsors. The ICC has no direct mechanism to sanction Dialog for ThePapare's advertising inventory. This is the regulatory gap: operator advertising falls between national gambling law (which has no enforcement precedent) and ICC code (which doesn't cover media companies).

Why This Matters Beyond Sri Lanka

The Melbet‚ÄìDialog‚ÄìThePapare arrangement illustrates a pattern replicated across South Asian cricket media. Offshore operators fund media ecosystems that depend on cricket governance bodies for rights ‚Äî creating mutual financial interests that make confrontation costly for all parties. Dialog cannot easily remove betting advertising without damaging ad revenue on a platform whose primary value proposition is cricket. SLC cannot easily confront Dialog without jeopardizing its sponsorship and broadcast relationship. The ICC cannot sanction Dialog without disrupting its own broadcast revenue chain in Sri Lanka. The result is structural inertia, not conspiracy ‚Äî which may be worse, because it is self-sustaining.

03 ‚Äî Kheloyar & the D-Company Connection

The LPL Sponsor Allegedly Linked to Dawood Ibrahim's Network

Among the six illegal operators that sponsored LPL franchises, Kheloyar carries the most serious documented allegations ‚Äî a direct line, via India's Enforcement Directorate, to the Mahadev Book network and an associate of India's most-wanted fugitive.

üîó The Mahadev Book Network

India's Enforcement Directorate, in its investigations into the Mahadev Book illegal betting syndicate, identified Kheloyar as operating within the same network. The Mahadev Book was built by Saurabh Chandrakar and Ravi Uppal, both reportedly based in Dubai while operating the platform targeting Indian bettors. The ED has attached over ‚Çπ500 crore in assets across its Mahadev Book investigation.

The specific connection investigators documented: Mustaqim Ibrahim Kaskar ‚Äî brother of Dawood Ibrahim Kaskar, the Mumbai crime lord on India's most-wanted list who is believed to operate primarily from Pakistan ‚Äî is alleged to have been involved in operating Kheloyar in partnership with the Mahadev Book network. Dawood Ibrahim's D-Company has historically been linked to match-fixing through the 2000 Hansie Cronje scandal and the 2010 spot-fixing investigations.

Why Babar Azam's Refusal Was Significant

When Babar Azam joined the Colombo Strikers for LPL 2023, he had inserted a specific clause in his contract forbidding him from wearing betting-related logos. After returning from Hajj, he appeared on the field in a blank jersey ‚Äî no Kheloyar logo, no 1xBook ‚Äî while every other player on both sides wore the sponsors. There was no press release, no statement from the PCB or LPL. Just a cricketer, alone among his peers, making a visible moral stand based on religious conviction. Given that Kheloyar is the operator the ED linked most directly to organised crime, Azam's refusal ‚Äî even if based purely on religious grounds ‚Äî was the most consequential act of integrity in the LPL's history.

üö® SLC's Position

Sri Lanka Cricket accepted Kheloyar as an LPL franchise sponsor. LPL marketing rights are controlled by IPG (Dubai-based), not SLC directly, but SLC administers the tournament and approved its commercial structure. The argument that SLC had no visibility into the operator's regulatory status or allegations ‚Äî given that India's ED investigation was public knowledge during the LPL 2023 period ‚Äî strains credibility.

No Sri Lankan official has publicly named Kheloyar's Mahadev Book connection. Sports Minister Ranasinghe named it as an illegal platform in Parliament, but the D-Company dimension has never entered Sri Lankan political discourse ‚Äî a notable silence given its gravity.

üåç The Broader Pattern

D-Company's involvement in cricket match-fixing is not new. The 2000 King Commission in South Africa documented links. The 2010 spot-fixing scandal in Pakistan cricket also produced allegations of D-Company involvement. The LPL's Kheloyar sponsorship, if the ED's documentation is accurate, represents a continuation of the same criminal ecosystem ‚Äî this time embedded not in the players' dressing room, but in the tournament's official commercial structure, on every jersey, broadcast to millions.

04 ‚Äî Wolf777

The Criminal Racket That Sponsored Sri Lanka Cricket

Wolf777 is not a grey-area offshore operator ‚Äî it is the subject of active criminal investigation in India, linked to hawala networks and local criminal agents. It appeared as a visible sponsor in Sri Lanka's premier domestic tournament.

üê∫ What Wolf777 Is

Wolf777 (also marketed as Wolf777News, Wbet, and related brands) operates through a network of local "agents" who facilitate deposits and withdrawals in cash, bypassing banking systems entirely ‚Äî a classic hawala-adjacent structure. India's Central Bureau of Investigation has investigated Wolf777 in connection with organised illegal betting syndicates. Unlike 1xBet which has at least a Cura√ßao registration providing nominal regulatory cover, Wolf777's operational structure is designed to be untraceable by design.

The platform's visibility in Sri Lanka has been primarily through social media promotions, WhatsApp group advertising, and ‚Äî during the LPL period ‚Äî jersey sponsorship on the Colombo Kings franchise. The absence of any banking infrastructure means Sri Lankan users transact in cash through local agents, making it impossible for either the Central Bank or the Gambling Regulatory Authority to track volume or enforce against it.

The Agent Network Model

Wolf777, like many South Asian illegal betting platforms, uses a tiered agent system. A "master agent" receives a cut of losses from sub-agents, who in turn receive a cut from end-users. Funds move through cash or cryptocurrency, never touching formal banking. This model is structurally resistant to the Central Bank's transaction monitoring because there are no transactions to monitor ‚Äî just cash. It also means that when the platform eventually collapses or is busted, the only people arrested are typically the lowest-level agents, while the platform operators remain offshore and untouchable.

üì∫ Media Complicity by Default

Wolf777's social media advertising in Sri Lanka has run on platforms including Facebook and Instagram, targeting cricket fans during match broadcasts. Meta's advertising platform does not proactively verify whether gambling operators hold licences in each jurisdiction where their ads run ‚Äî advertisers self-certify compliance. This means Wolf777 can legally (by Meta's ad policies) target Sri Lankan users despite being an unlicensed and criminally investigated operator, because Meta's system has no mechanism to verify Sri Lankan gambling law compliance at the point of ad approval. Sri Lankan regulators have no direct enforcement mechanism against a US-listed company for ads placed from servers outside Sri Lanka.

05 ‚Äî The Celebrity Endorsement Machine

How the Network Bought Legitimacy Through Famous Faces

When you attach a beloved cricketer's face to a brand, millions of fans assume the brand is legitimate. India's Enforcement Directorate attached assets from cricketers who endorsed 1xBet-affiliated platforms ‚Äî confirming the strategy was knowingly exploited, not merely an oversight.

‚≠ê The Strategy

The endorsement strategy was deliberate and systematic: sign recognisable cricketers and entertainment celebrities for promotional contracts, run their faces in social media ads, and use their credibility to recruit ordinary bettors. For operators that are illegal in their target markets, celebrity association serves a second function ‚Äî it creates political risk for any regulator who tries to act, because shutting down a platform endorsed by a national icon becomes a story about the icon, not the illegality.

India's Enforcement Directorate, in its investigations into 1xBet-affiliated platforms and the Mahadev Book network, attached assets from or investigated multiple prominent cricketers and celebrities. 06 ‚Äî Six Layers of Enablers

How the Network Survived Every Attempt to Dismantle It

The betting network did not survive through brute corruption alone. It survived because it embedded itself across six mutually reinforcing layers of institutional enablement ‚Äî each layer with its own incentives to maintain the status quo.

  • 01

    Sri Lanka Cricket's Revenue Dependency

    SLC's budget depends heavily on LPL franchise fees (USD 2.5 million per team) and tournament commercial revenue. With six franchises, that is a USD 15 million revenue base per edition. The operators who provide this via franchise sponsorship cannot be removed without collapsing the tournament's commercial model. SLC's dire financial position during the 2021‚Äì2023 economic crisis made this dependency acute: the board needed the money, regardless of where it came from.

  • 02

    The IPG Firewall

    LPL's marketing and commercial rights sit with IPG, a Dubai-based company of Indian origin. By delegating commercial decisions to an offshore entity, SLC created legal and regulatory distance: the betting operators are technically IPG's commercial partners, not SLC's direct sponsors. When Sri Lankan regulators or parliamentarians raise questions, the answer is that the arrangements are contractual obligations made by IPG, outside Sri Lankan jurisdiction. This is the same firewall structure used by the Board of Control for Cricket in India (BCCI) in early IPL editions ‚Äî deliberate offshore commercialisation that insulates the domestic board from regulatory accountability.

  • 03

    Political Entanglement

    Sri Lanka Cricket's board elections are political events. Every government since 2004 has attempted to control SLC's board, either through election influence or ministerial dissolution. This political entanglement means that anti-corruption reform requires political will ‚Äî but every minister who has exercised that will (Fernando, Ranasinghe, Rajapaksa) has been removed, transferred, or overridden. The ICC suspended SLC in November 2023 for "government interference" ‚Äî the intervention that was most likely to clean up the board ‚Äî which paradoxically shielded the board by invoking ICC's constitutional protections against state interference.

  • 04

    Media Ecosystem Capture

    The primary cricket media platform (ThePapare) is owned by the primary cricket broadcast rights holder (Dialog). Independent cricket journalism in Sri Lanka is thin: most cricket-adjacent media rely on access to SLC officials, LPL credentials, and advertising relationships with the same sponsors. Investigative journalism about LPL betting has been largely imported from Indian outlets (Cricket Age, ESPN Cricinfo investigations) or international media. Sri Lankan media has generally avoided the Kheloyar‚ÄìD-Company and Melbet‚ÄìDialog dimensions of the story entirely.

  • 05

    Regulatory Immaturity

    The Gambling Regulatory Authority was only established in 2025. Before that, Sri Lanka had no dedicated gambling regulator. Police enforcement under older statutes required proof of physical gambling premises ‚Äî impossible to apply to Cura√ßao-registered online platforms. The 2025 Act gives the Authority powers over online promotion, but the Authority lacks the technical capacity (blockchain tracing, IP attribution, international regulatory cooperation) to act against platforms operated from offshore jurisdictions with minimal disclosure requirements. Building that capacity takes years and sustained political support ‚Äî neither of which has been guaranteed.

  • 06

    The ICC's Blind Spot

    The ICC Anti-Corruption Code applies to players, officials, and administrators ‚Äî not to media companies, telecom sponsors, or tournament commercial partners. The ICC's own commercial relationships (such as the broadcast rights held by Dialog, which is the parent company of an entity carrying illegal betting advertising) create conflicts that the ICC's governance structure is not designed to resolve. The ICC has a Colombo ACU office that has handled more simultaneous corruption investigations than any other territory ‚Äî but its mandate is player and official corruption, not systemic commercial corruption at the board and sponsor level.

The loop has no natural exit point. Reform requires simultaneous political will, media pressure, regulatory capacity, and ICC support ‚Äî a combination that has never materialised together.

üîÑ How It Survived Three Reform Attempts

Attempt 1 (Fernando): Filed the ICC report on Sumathipala, dissolved the board. Result: ICC reinstated the board, Fernando was eventually moved from the portfolio. Attempt 2 (Ranasinghe): Named 'Betting Shammi' in Parliament, dissolved the board again. Result: Wickremesinghe fired him from all ministerial positions. Attempt 3 (NPP Government 2024): Promised restructuring, appointed Arjuna Ranatunga to oversee reform. Result: Ranatunga's proposals have not produced a substantive change in board composition or commercial structure.

üí° What Would Break the Loop

Based on how similar loops have been broken in other cricket jurisdictions: the loop breaks either through external pressure that removes the ICC's tolerance (a match-fixing conviction that embarrasses the ICC globally), through media pressure that creates a sustained public narrative, or through a government with sufficient institutional security to absorb the ICC's "interference" threat. The ICC's suspension in 2023 ‚Äî rather than breaking the loop ‚Äî became a mechanism for preserving it, by making government intervention legally problematic under ICC statutes.

The Bottom Line

The conviction record is extensive. The structural corruption is documented. Three ministers have tried to intervene and been removed. The ICC's ACU has more permanent staff in Colombo than anywhere outside Dubai ‚Äî and more simultaneous active investigations. The Auditor General's reports document financial irregularities. A franchise owner has pleaded guilty to match-fixing. And yet: the same board remains. The same commercial structure continues. The same illegal operators advertise on the same platforms. The same players who grew up watching this institution fail them line up to represent a country that let them down at every level. What happened against New Zealand on February 27, 2026 was not a bad day. It was the inevitable output of a system that has been deliberately designed to produce it.
