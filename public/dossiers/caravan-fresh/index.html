The Caravan Fresh "Chicken Cheese Bomb" Incident

කැරවෑන් ෆ්‍රෙෂ් නම් බේකරි දෙපළ බෝම්බ ඉමෝජි සහ සිංහල පෙළ භාවිතා කරමින් කෑ ඩෙලිවරි නිෂ්පාදිතයක් ඇලවුවේය — ගාසාවේ එක්සත් ජනපද/ඊශ්‍රායල් බෝම්බ ප්‍රහාරවලින් පාසල් දැරියන් මරා දමන අවස්ථාවක, ශ්‍රී ලංකාවේ සිවිල් යුද්ධ තුවාල තවමත් සුව නොවූ සමාජයක. වෙළඳ ප්‍රචාරණයේ බෝම්බ රූපක භාවිතය පිළිබඳ පුළුල් විවේචනයට ලක් විය.

Caravan Fresh published a bakery delivery product ad using bomb emojis and Sinhala text that evoked the ongoing US/Israeli bombing of Gaza — where schoolgirls and children were being killed — while also reopening old wounds from Sri Lanka's own civil wars. The post triggered widespread criticism over the casual use of bombing imagery in commercial marketing.

The Incident

කැරවෑන් ෆ්‍රෙෂ් පිටුවෙහි, බේකරි දෙපළ වෙතින් "චිකන් චීස් බෝම්බ්" නම් නිෂ්පාදිතයක් පිළිබඳ වෙළඳ දැන්වීමක් ප්‍රකාශයට පත් කරන ලදී. එම පෝස්ටුවේ බෝම්බ ඉමෝජි (💣) සහ සිංහල පෙළ භාවිතා කරන ලදී — ගාසාවේ එක්සත් ජනපද/ඊශ්‍රායල් බෝම්බ ප්‍රහාරවලින් පාසල් දැරියන් ඇතුළු ළමුන් මරා දමන අවස්ථාවක, එමෙන්ම ශ්‍රී ලංකාවේ සිවිල් යුද්ධවල තුවාල තවමත් සුව නොවූ සමාජයක, මෙය ප්‍රේක්ෂකයන්ට දරුණු ලෙස අසංවේදී ලෙස දැනුණි.

Caravan Fresh published an advertisement for a bakery delivery product called "Chicken Cheese Bomb." The post used bomb emojis (💣) and Sinhala text that, amid the ongoing US/Israeli bombing of Gaza — where schoolgirls and children were being killed — and in a society still carrying the scars of Sri Lanka's civil wars, struck audiences as grotesquely tone-deaf.

පෝස්ටුවට ප්‍රතිචාර ලෙස 3,800+ "angry" reactions සහ 218+ comments ලැබුණි. යුද්ධ රූපක භාවිතය ගැන පෙන්වා දුන් අය official page එකෙන් block කරන ලදී.

The post received 3,800+ "angry" reactions and 218+ comments of criticism. Those who pointed out the inappropriate use of war imagery found themselves blocked from the official page.

Legal Detail
Criminal Defamation — Full Context
  • OS
    Penal Code (Amendment) Act No. 12 of 2002 — Sections 479–482 (criminal defamation) were repealed by the Sri Lankan Parliament, making Sri Lanka the first country in South Asia to decriminalize defamation. This means no person can face criminal prosecution — arrest, detention, or imprisonment — for expressing opinions about a company, product, or public figure.
  • FB
    Relevance to this case: When Caravan Fresh's official statement threatened "legal action" against critics, many consumers feared criminal consequences. Under Sri Lankan law, this threat could only refer to civil defamation — a lawsuit for monetary damages, not criminal prosecution. The phrasing was arguably designed to intimidate consumers unfamiliar with the 2002 repeal.
Legal status Confirmed — Act No. 12/2002
Sri Lanka's criminal defamation repeal is internationally recognized. Article 19 (UK) and Reporters Without Borders have both cited it as a landmark reform. No subsequent government has attempted re-criminalization.

Civil Defamation Defenses

Valid defenses: (1) Truth/justification, (2) Fair comment on matters of public interest.

Legal Detail
Civil Defamation Defenses — Full Context
  • OS
    Truth / Justification (Veritas) — If a statement is substantially true, it is a complete defense to civil defamation. In this case, every claim in public posts was backed by screenshots of Caravan Fresh's own content. Truth does not require perfection — substantial truth is sufficient.
  • OS
    Fair Comment — Honest opinion on matters of public interest is protected even if the opinion is harsh, exaggerated, or prejudiced — provided it is based on true facts and is recognizable as opinion (not presented as fact). Criticism of a company's marketing campaign is textbook fair comment.
  • OS
    Qualified Privilege — Statements made in good faith, where the speaker has a duty or interest to communicate and the recipient has a corresponding interest in receiving the information. Consumer warnings about insensitive corporate behavior likely qualify.
Application All three defenses apply here
Every public criticism of Caravan Fresh documented in this dossier was (a) based on verifiable screenshots, (b) recognizable as opinion/commentary, and (c) concerned a matter of public interest. A civil defamation suit against these critics would face all three defenses simultaneously.

Public Interest Doctrine

Consumers can express opinions about products and corporate practices as a matter of public interest.

Legal Detail
Public Interest — Full Context
  • OS
    Article 14(1)(a) of the Constitution — Guarantees freedom of speech and expression to every citizen of Sri Lanka. This is a fundamental right that can only be restricted by law "in the interests of racial and religious harmony or in relation to parliamentary privilege, contempt of court, defamation or incitement to an offence."
  • OS
    Consumer Protection Authority Act No. 1 of 2003 — Established the Consumer Affairs Authority with powers to investigate unfair trade practices, misleading advertising, and conduct prejudicial to consumers. Consumers reporting corporate misconduct are exercising rights explicitly created by this statute.
  • FB
    Relevance: A multinational bakery chain using war/conflict imagery to sell food during an active security situation is a matter of public interest. The company has 69+ outlets across Western Province, affecting tens of thousands of consumers. Public commentary on their marketing practices is constitutionally protected speech.
Legal basis Constitutional + Statutory
Both constitutional guarantees (Art. 14) and the Consumer Protection Authority Act create a strong legal framework for consumer expression about corporate practices. Companies threatening legal action against consumer critics must overcome these protections.

Online Safety Act 2024

Controversial new law criticized by UN, EU, and civil society for potential restrictions on legitimate expression.

Legal Detail
Online Safety Act — Full Context
  • OS
    Online Safety Act No. 9 of 2024 — Enacted January 2024. Creates an Online Safety Commission with broad powers to issue takedown orders and restrict online content. Defines "prohibited statements" including those causing "feelings of ill-will" between communities. Criticized for vague language that could criminalize legitimate consumer criticism and journalism.
  • OS
    International criticism: The UN High Commissioner for Human Rights called it "incompatible with international human rights law." The EU Delegation to Sri Lanka expressed "deep concern." Reporters Without Borders ranked it a threat to press freedom. The Bar Association of Sri Lanka challenged its constitutionality (Supreme Court upheld with amendments).
  • FB
    Could Caravan Fresh use this law? Theoretically, a company could file a complaint under the Act claiming online posts caused "reputational harm." However, (a) the Act was primarily designed for cyberbullying and false information, not consumer criticism backed by evidence; (b) truth remains a defense; (c) any complaint would need to show the content was "false" — difficult when evidence consists of the company's own screenshots.
Risk assessment Low risk — but monitor
While the Online Safety Act theoretically creates new avenues for corporate complaints, evidence-based consumer reporting is unlikely to meet the threshold for "prohibited statements." The Act's application is still being tested in courts. Several constitutional challenges are ongoing. Consumers should document everything (as done in this dossier) to maintain defensibility.

Business Hotline Calls

Calling a business's public hotline to voice concerns is a legitimate consumer action, not harassment.

Legal Detail
Hotline Calls — Full Context
  • FB
    The company's claim: Kavindi Athapaththu stated that "certain individuals (men) had called our hotline and harassed our female customer care staff" [IMG_6]. The official statement referenced "organized groups" targeting their business.
  • FB
    The rebuttal: Dewyan Thilakasiri responded: "Criticism of a brand's public post is not harassment. Sharing a publicly listed business hotline is not harassment. If your hotline was overwhelmed, that's a reflection of how many people were genuinely affected." [IMG_25] — This distinction is legally sound.
  • OS
    Legal analysis: Calling a business hotline to express dissatisfaction is a standard consumer action. For it to constitute "harassment" under Sri Lankan law (Penal Code Section 345), there would need to be evidence of repeated calls with intent to cause annoyance — by the same person. Multiple different consumers each calling once is not harassment; it is volume of complaint reflecting public sentiment. The gendered framing ("men calling female staff") introduces irrelevant emotional context to delegitimize consumer action.
Verdict Consumer calls ≠ harassment
A publicly listed business hotline exists for customer interaction. When many consumers call to express displeasure about a corporate action, that is the hotline functioning as intended. Characterizing this as "harassment" conflates volume of consumer response with individual misconduct — and the gendered framing attempts to weaponize gender sensitivity to silence legitimate criticism.
