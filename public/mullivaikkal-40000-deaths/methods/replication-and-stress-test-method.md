# Replication And Stress-Test Method

## Purpose

This method lets another researcher inspect whether a public claim preserves the source record or hardens it.

The focus is not motive. The focus is wording, source support, category discipline, and propagation.

## Step 1: Capture The Exact Claim

Record:

- exact wording
- number used
- geography
- time window
- category: killed, injured, missing, disappeared, unaccounted, demographic discrepancy, legal recognition, or memorial shorthand
- source cited
- page URL
- access date

## Step 2: Identify The Claimed Source

Ask:

- Does the page cite a primary source?
- Does it cite a derivative source?
- Is it citing Wikipedia, a mirror, a media article, an NGO statement, a political motion, or a UN/OHCHR source?
- Is the source attached to the exact sentence or only listed generally?

## Step 3: Find The Strongest Upstream Source

For the `40,000` claim, the strongest current source route is the 2011 UN Panel's cautious upper-estimate wording for final-phase civilian deaths.

Any stronger public wording must show a stronger source.

## Step 4: Check For Claim Hardening

Look for:

- `up to` removed
- `could not be ruled out` changed to `were killed`
- `final phase / Vanni` changed to `Mullivaikkal alone`
- `unaccounted` changed to `killed`
- `political recognition` treated as legal adjudication
- `UN-hosted NGO submission` treated as a UN finding
- `mirror/copy page` treated as independent corroboration

## Step 5: Assign A Publication-Safety Signal

Use these labels:

- `Source supports this`: directly supported with category, geography, and time window preserved
- `Needs qualifier`: support exists only when the qualifier, category, geography, and time window are visible
- `Repeated source`: repeats another source; useful for propagation, not original proof
- `Mixed categories`: blurs deaths, missing, unaccounted, disappeared, legal recognition, or geography
- `Not supported as written`: not publishable in its current wording because it outruns the source
- `Needs checking`: exact wording, source chain, or provenance still needs capture
- `Broken or missing link`: 404, removed, inaccessible, or not enough text captured

## Step 6: Stress-Test The Counter-Claim

Before rebutting a claim, ask:

- Does the rebuttal erase civilian suffering?
- Does it treat official lower counts as neutral ceilings?
- Does it infer motive from identity or institutional affiliation?
- Does it accuse funding or coordination without money-to-message evidence?
- Does it preserve the distinction between death toll, missing persons, disappearance, and unaccounted categories?

## Step 7: Write The Safe Correction

A good correction has this structure:

1. What the original source supports.
2. What the public claim adds or changes.
3. What remains uncertain.
4. What accountability still requires.

Example:

> Current evidence does not verify `40,000 deaths in Mullivaikkal alone`. The source trail points to a caveated final-phase Vanni casualty figure: overwhelmingly Tamil civilians in context, but not an exact count, not a Mullivaikkal-only death toll, and not by itself proof of legal intent. Accountability still requires a credible record for deaths, missing persons, disappearances, and state/LTTE conduct in the final phase.
