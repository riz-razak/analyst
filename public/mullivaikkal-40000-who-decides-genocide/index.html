Words before numbers

Mullivaikkal 40,000: Who Decides Genocide?

A plain-language guide to the difference between memory language, recognition language, investigative allegation, and legal determination.

The short answer

Communities and political bodies can use genocide language as memory or recognition. Investigators can document evidence and patterns. Courts or legally final tribunals decide genocide as a criminal/legal finding for specific facts.

OHCHR is not the court that convicts people of genocide. It is important because it publishes and applies the UN human-rights/treaty framework, including the Genocide Convention; legal decision-making sits with competent courts such as the ICC for individual criminal responsibility where jurisdiction exists, and the ICJ for disputes between States under the Convention.

That distinction matters because the phrase `40,000 died in Mullivaikkal alone` has not been established in this dataset as a final legal finding with that exact number and geography.

The authority ladder

Memory laneCommunity and remembranceFamilies, survivors, diaspora groups, and civic movements can use strong moral language to describe grief and injury.
Evidence laneInvestigative recordReports, testimony, hospital evidence, population estimates, and accountability inquiries can establish allegations and patterns.
Legal laneJudicial determinationA competent court or final legal tribunal applies legal standards, including intent, evidence, jurisdiction, and due process.

Why this matters for SEO

Search engines do not naturally distinguish between a memorial statement, a parliamentary recognition statement, a legal advocacy brief, a UN report, and a court judgment. If all five use similar language, the search result can make repetition feel like proof.

The correction is not to erase the word. The correction is to label the lane before the reader absorbs the conclusion.

The Sinhala-language layer adds another risk. A technical route exists for ethnic cleansing as `වාර්ගික විශුද්ධිකරණය` / `ජනවාර්ගික පාරිශුද්ධකරණය`, but that does not mean average public debate clearly separates ethnic cleansing from `සංහාරය`, massacre, or genocide. When these terms collapse, a specific allegation about state conduct can be heard by the Sinhala/Sri Lankan south as a charge against the whole community.

  • Use `genocide` as legal fact only where a competent legal determination directly supports that use.
  • Use political-recognition language as political-recognition language.
  • Use community memory language as memory language.
  • Use investigative evidence as evidence, not as completed legal adjudication.
  • Use ethnic-cleansing language as forced-removal/cleansing language, not as automatic genocide.

Safe public wording

Use:

The evidence raises grave accountability questions and includes serious allegations about civilian harm in the final phase. But the number `40,000 in Mullivaikkal alone` is not verified as stated, and genocide language must be labelled by source type unless a competent legal determination is being cited.
